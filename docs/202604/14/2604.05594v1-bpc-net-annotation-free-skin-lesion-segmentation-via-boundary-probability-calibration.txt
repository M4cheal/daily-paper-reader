A B S T R A C T 

Annotation-free skin lesion segmentation is attractive for low-resource dermoscopic deployment. However, its performance remains constrained by three coupled challenges: noisy pseudo-label supervision, unstable transfer under limited target-domain data, and boundary probability under-confidence. Most existing annotation-free methods primarily focus on pseudo-label denoising. In contrast, the effect of compressed boundary probabilities on final mask quality has received less explicit attention, although it directly affects contour completeness and cannot be adequately corrected by global threshold adjustment alone. To address this issue, we propose a boundary probability calibration framework for annotation-free skin lesion segmentation. The core of the framework is Gaussian Probability Smoothing (GPS), which performs localized probability-space calibration before thresholding to recover under-confident lesion boundaries without inducing indiscriminate foreground expansion. To support this calibration under noisy pseudo-supervision and cross-domain transfer, we further incorporate two auxiliary designs: a feature-decoupled decoder that separately handles context suppression, detail recovery, and boundary refinement, and an interaction-branch adaptation strategy that updates only the pseudo-label interaction branch while preserving the deployed image-only segmentation path. Under a strictly annotation-free protocol, no manual masks are used during training or target-domain adaptation, and validation labels, when available, are used only for final operating-point selection. Experiments on ISIC-2017, ISIC-2018, and PH2 show that the proposed framework achieves state-of-the-art performance among published unsupervised methods, reaching a macro-average Dice coefficient and Jaccard index of 85.80% and 76.97%, respectively, while approaching supervised reference performance on PH2. 

## 1. Introduction 

Automated skin lesion segmentation is an important component of computer-aided dermoscopic analysis. How-ever, acquiring accurate pixel-level lesion masks remains ex-pensive, labor-intensive, and difficult to scale across datasets and clinical sites. This limitation is particularly restric-tive in low-resource or cross-center deployment scenarios, where repeated expert annotation is often impractical. Con-sequently, annotation-free skin lesion segmentation has be-come an increasingly important research direction. Recent annotation-free and unsupervised dermoscopic segmentation methods have improved lesion localization through clustering and saliency priors (Patiño et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2023b; Shi and Malik, 2000; MacQueen, 1967; Zhou et al., 2023a). Self-supervised and uncertainty-aware variants have further improved pseudo-label quality and reliability (Ahn et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2023; Li et al., 2026, 2024). Nevertheless, annotation-free segmentation contin-ues to face three tightly coupled challenges. First, pseudo-labels remain noisy, particularly around low-contrast lesions and ambiguous lesion boundaries. Second, under conser-vative pseudo-supervision, predicted boundary probabilities are often compressed, resulting in under-confident lesion

Third, when only limited target-domain data are available, cross-domain transfer becomes unstable because the reliability pattern of pseudo-label supervision changes across datasets. Collectively, these factors limit lesion structure learning, boundary preservation, and deployment robustness. Among these issues, pseudo-label noise has received the greatest attention, whereas boundary probability under-confidence has been discussed less explicitly. In practice, however, final segmentation quality is often strongly influ-enced by this factor. Even when lesion interiors are reason-ably identified, compressed boundary probabilities may still lead to incomplete contours, weak boundary responses, and suboptimal overlap metrics after thresholding. A straight-forward remedy is to lower the decision threshold. Such threshold relaxation, however, is a global operation: it shifts the decision boundary of all pixels simultaneously and typ-ically improves sensitivity at the cost of substantial false-positive expansion. Therefore, a key question is not only how to denoise pseudo-labels during training, but also how to calibrate boundary probabilities more effectively during inference under annotation-free learning. In this work, we address annotation-free dermoscopic segmentation from the perspective of boundary probability calibration. We propose a unified framework centered on Gaussian Probability Smoothing (GPS), which performs localized probability-space calibration before thresholding. To make this calibration effective under noisy pseudo-supervision and limited target-domain transfer, we introduce two auxiliary designs. Experiments on ISIC-2017, ISIC-2018, and PH2 show that the proposed framework achieves state-of-the-art performance among published unsupervised methods and approaches supervised performance on PH2. More importantly, both ablation results and operating-frontier analysis indicate that the contribution of GPS is not equivalent to selecting a different threshold on the same out-put distribution. Instead, GPS alters the effective boundary decision frontier through localized probability calibration. The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows: 1. We identify boundary probability under-confidence as a distinct and underexplored bottleneck in annotation-free skin lesion segmentation, and propose a boundary probability calibration framework centered on Gaussian Probability Smoothing (GPS) to explicitly address this problem. 2. We further introduce a feature-decoupled decoder and an interaction-branch adaptation strategy as supporting mechanisms to enable effective boundary calibration un-der noisy pseudo-label supervision and limited target-domain transfer. 3. Extensive experiments on three public dermoscopic benchmarks demonstrate that the proposed framework achieves state-of-the-art performance among published unsupervised methods while preserving a practical annotation-free deployment setting. 

## 2. Related Work 

2.1. Unsupervised Skin Lesion Segmentation 

Early unsupervised dermoscopic segmentation relied mainly on low-level grouping cues. Patiño et al. (2018) merged superpixels under hand-crafted similarity assump-tions, while K-means (MacQueen, 1967) and NCut (Shi and Malik, 2000) represent the classical clustering family that partitions pixels or features without semantic supervision. Later methods injected stronger lesion priors. Saliency-CCE (Zhou et al., 2023b) couples colour-context extraction with saliency reasoning, and texture-guided saliency distilling (Zhou et al., 2023a) shows that richer appearance cues can sharpen unsupervised foreground localization. More recent approaches improve the feature space before mask extrac-tion. Ahn et al. (2021) learn spatially guided self-supervised clustering features, whereas Zeng et al. (2023) impose lesion structure by minimizing structural entropy on multi-scale superpixel graphs. Classical refiners such as dense CRF (Krähenbühl and Koltun, 2011) and guided filtering (He et al., 2013) are also widely used to sharpen coarse bound-aries, but they are typically added as generic postprocessing rather than being tied to an explicit reliability model. Recent uncertainty-aware dermoscopic methods, notably RPI-Net (Li et al., 2026) and USL-Net (Li et al., 2024), further improve pseudo-label quality, yet their emphasis remains denoising within a single domain. Our framework inherits reliability-aware pseudo-label interaction as one compo-nent, but extends the problem to cross-domain transfer and inference-stage boundary calibration. 

2.2. Cross-Domain Transfer and Uncertainty Modeling 

Cross-domain medical segmentation has been studied from several angles. Karani et al. (2021) adapt segmentation networks at test time to improve robustness without full retraining. Chen et al. (2019) align images and features to reduce modality gaps, while Liu et al. (2020) use shape-aware meta-learning to preserve anatomical structure un-der unseen-domain shift. In a stricter deployment setting, Bateson et al. (2022) remove source data entirely and per-form source-free adaptation from a pretrained model. To-gether, these studies show that domain shift can be mit-igated, but many solutions still update a substantial part of the deployed segmentation network or introduce rela-tively heavy alignment machinery. In parallel, uncertainty modeling has become a standard tool for discounting noisy supervision and reweighting ambiguous pixels. Within der-moscopy, uncertainty-aware annotation-free learning has likewise been used to reweight unreliable pseudo-label paths and stabilize self-learning (Li et al., 2026, 2024). Our setting lies at the intersection of these two lines of work, but differs in what is adapted: instead of updating the deployed image-only path, we freeze it and recalibrate only the pseudo-label interaction branch. This design reflects our hypothesis that the dominant cross-domain shift in annotation-free trans-fer lies in pseudo-label reliability topology rather than in

2.3. Foundation Models and Lightweight Visual Backbones 

Foundation-model-based medical segmentation has ex-panded rapidly after SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023). MedSAM (Ma et al., 2024) shows that promptable segmentation can transfer effectively to medical images after domain-specific adaptation, while SAMed (Zhang and Liu, 2023) and SAM-Med2D (Cheng et al., 2023) further tailor the SAM family to medical fine-tuning and broader 2D medical coverage. These models are important references, but they target a different operating regime from ours because prompts, extra supervision, or heavier fine-tuning are usually part of the workflow. We therefore treat them as contextual reference methods rather than direct annotation-free counterparts. On the backbone side, ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022) shows that modern ConvNet design can provide stronger hierar-chical features than legacy CNN encoders. This matters here because weak representations amplify pseudo-label noise and limit lesion-boundary recovery. Nevertheless, a stronger encoder alone does not resolve the coupled prob-lems of noisy pseudo-label supervision, cross-domain re-liability shift, and boundary probability compression. We therefore use ConvNeXt-Tiny as a stronger image backbone, while placing the main methodological emphasis on pseudo-label interaction, parameter-decoupled adaptation, and GPS.

3.8. Interaction-Branch Adaptation 

Stage 2 addresses cross-domain uncertainty shift by partitioning the model into a frozen deployed image-only segmentation path and a trainable pseudo-label interaction branch. The frozen set contains the ConvNeXt-Tiny en-coder, decoder/refinement modules, segmentation head, and auxiliary boundary/detail/uncertainty heads. The trainable set contains only the pseudo-label encoder, IPC/PIA mod-ules, and 𝜎 heads, i.e., 1.056 M of 31.42 M parameters (3.36%). The rationale is that annotation-free cross-domain degradation is driven mainly by changes in pseudo-label reliability and disagreement structure, not by a collapse of generic lesion semantics in the image backbone. Fully updating the deployed image-only path on a small target set would therefore risk imprinting target-domain pseudo-label errors onto the mapping used at test time. By restrict-ing adaptation to pseudo-label encoding, interaction, and uncertainty weighting, Stage 2 changes how noisy target-domain supervision is interpreted during optimization while preserving the source-trained image-to-mask function used at inference. The decoder is a supporting architectural design aimed at stabilizing lesion-mask learning prior to boundary cal-ibration, rather than a stand-alone theoretical contribu-tion. We separate three competing requirements of lesion segmentation—context suppression, detail recovery, and boundary refinement—into dedicated lightweight modules. ## 4. Experiments 

4.1. Experimental Protocol 

We evaluate on three public dermoscopy datasets: 

• ISIC-2017 (Codella et al., 2018): 2,000 training / 150 validation / 600 test images. 

• ISIC-2018 (Codella et al., 2019): 2,594 training / 100 validation / 1,000 test images. 

• PH2 (Mendonça et al., 2013): 200 images total; randomly split with seed=42 at ratios (0.70, 0.15, 0.15) into 140 training / 30 validation / 30 test images. All experiments are implemented in PyTorch with AdamW ( 𝛽 1 = 0.9, 𝛽 2 = 0.999 , weight decay 10 −4 ), gradient clipping at 1.0, and input resolution 224 × 224 .Training augmentation is intentionally minimal—random horizontal and vertical flips only—because stronger photo-metric or geometric perturbations destabilize the contrast structure and spatial correspondence assumed by automatic pseudo-label generation. Source-domain training on ISIC-2017 is performed from scratch with learning rate 6 × 10 −6 ,batch size 16, 24 epochs, and one iterative pseudo-label refinement round (8 epochs, blend ratio 0.35). For target-domain adaptation on ISIC-2018 and PH2, the best Stage 1 checkpoint is reused, the deployed image-only path is frozen, and only the pseudo_encoder, IPC/PIA modules, and 

𝜎 heads are updated (1.056 M / 31.42 M parameters). This second stage uses learning rate 5 × 10 −6 , batch size 12, and 2 epochs without iterative refinement. Key differences between the two stages are summarized in Table 2. Table 3 reports model complexity and runtime statistics of the proposed framework. Measurements were obtained on an NVIDIA RTX 4060 Ti at input resolution 224 × 224 . The model contains 31.42 M parameters and runs at 87.4 FPS on the deployed image-only path, corresponding to 29.9% of the RPI-Net parameter count (Li et al., 2026). We report standard overlap metrics (ACC, DICE, JAC, SEN, SPE) and boundary distance metrics (HD95, ASSD). Metrics are computed per test image and then averaged over the test set. For HD95/ASSD, empty-empty pairs are assigned 0, whereas one-empty pairs receive a diagonal-length penalty. Operating points are determined by validation-set grid search and held fixed for all test evaluations (Table 4): The unusually low threshold 𝜏 = 0 .02 on ISIC-2017 reflects score calibration under DUL: lesion logits on ISIC-2017 are more compressed, so foreground probabilities peak well below 1.0. Figure 5 shows the foreground-background valley below 0.05 on ISIC-2017, placing 𝜏 = 0 .02 inside the natural separation region; ISIC-2018 and PH2 instead shift to higher foreground medians (0.947 and 0.955) and therefore use higher thresholds (0.13 and 0.10). The test set is never used for operating-point se-lection. For reference, the untuned P0 operating point (𝜏 =0.30, no post-processing) already yields DICE=78.81% and JAC=68.44% on ISIC-2017, confirming that 𝜏 =0.02 is performance-refining rather than performance-enabling. ## 5. Discussion 

Our framework suggests that annotation-free dermo-scopic segmentation can approach supervised quality when GPS and interaction-branch adaptation are used within a clearly delimited protocol. The contribution of GPS lies in linking local probability diffusion to boundary-probability compression in annotation-free training, rather than in the Gaussian kernel alone. The effectiveness of GPS follows directly from the 1D boundary model derived in Section 3.10. Lowering the threshold 𝜏 → 𝜏 − 𝛿 is a global operation—every pixel’s decision boundary shifts simultaneously, causing ΔFP ∝ 𝑆 

and thus an approximately linear SEN–SPE trade-off. GPS instead acts as a local probability-space calibration step: the Gaussian kernel produces meaningful diffusion only near high-probability lesion boundaries, while background pix-els far from lesions—already near-zero probability—remain essentially unchanged after convolution. This changes the operating frontier in a way that can improve SEN at compar-atively small SPE cost within our inference pipeline. Eq. 16 gives the corresponding GPS efficiency ratio. 

|ΔSEN ||ΔSPE | ≈ 𝑆 − 𝑆 fg 

𝑆 fg 

(16) In dermoscopic images, lesions typically span 10%–30% of image area, predicting efficiency ratios of 2.3:1– 9:1—consistent with ablation observations: on ISIC-2017 (P2 →P3), SEN gains +0.95 pp while SPE drops only 0.16 pp (ratio ≈6:1, lesion area ≈14%); on PH2 the ratio is ≈2:1 (lesion area ≈33%). The sub-linear growth of Δ𝑥 with 𝜎 

further improves hyperparameter stability. Figure 9 provides the key inference-side evidence: under this pipeline, GPS moves the observed SEN–SPE frontier upward and right-ward, consistent with local boundary expansion rather than indiscriminate global threshold relaxation. The fact that the source-trained framework achieves higher final DICE on PH2 (92.14%) than on ISIC-2017 (81.03%) is not paradoxical. PH2 images generally exhibit clearer lesion boundaries, larger foreground extent, and less distracting background structure, which reduces intrinsic boundary ambiguity relative to ISIC-2017. Our interaction-branch adaptation is designed precisely for this regime: the frozen backbone preserves lesion semantics learned from the larger source domain, while the trainable uncertainty-sensitive heads recalibrate the model to the target-domain noise topology and output distribution. As a result, cross-domain transfer can outperform in-domain source perfor-mance when the target domain is structurally cleaner and its label-noise structure is easier to align. Several limitations remain. Residual failures are con-centrated in cases with severe hair occlusion, extremely low lesion-to-skin contrast, or very gradual boundary tran-sitions, where noisy initialization can still induce localized false negatives or occasional false positives near ambiguous borders. The darkness/centroid/area priors used in pseudo-label initialization are dermoscopy-specific and would likely need replacement in modalities with different foreground appearance or framing. Evaluation is still confined to public dermoscopy benchmarks and pixel-level overlap metrics, so broader cross-center validation and multi-expert boundary assessment remain open. Statistical inference is strongest for within-study ablations; comparisons to prior literature are therefore reported as benchmark-level margins plus con-fidence intervals of our method rather than paired case-wise tests. GPS also still requires validation-stage operating-point search, although the no-target-label controls in Supple-mentary Table S1 support the view that this search refines performance rather than enables it; notably, even the untuned P0 default on ISIC-2017 already yields 78.81% DICE and 68.44% JAC, indicating a strong performance floor before validation-stage refinement. 

## 6. Conclusion 

We presented a boundary probability calibration frame-work for annotation-free skin lesion segmentation, cen-tered on Gaussian Probability Smoothing (GPS). By iden-tifying boundary probability under-confidence as a dis-tinct bottleneck under pseudo-label learning, GPS per-forms localized probability-space calibration to recover le-sion contours without indiscriminate foreground expansion. Interaction-branch adaptation and feature-decoupled decod-ing are integrated as supporting mechanisms that stabilize noisy pseudo-supervision and limited target-domain trans-fer. Across ISIC-2017, ISIC-2018, and PH2, the frame-work reaches macro-average DICE/JAC of 85.80%/76.97% and surpasses 11 prior unsupervised baselines, supporting annotation-free dermoscopic segmentation as a practical direction for low-resource deployment.
