# Learning–Inference Concurrency in DynamicGate-MLP: Structural and Mathematical Justification

Abstract 

Conventional neural networks strictly separate learning and inference because if parame-ters are updated during inference, outputs become unstable and even the inference function itself is not well-defined [1, 2, 3]. This paper shows that DynamicGate-MLP structurally permits learning–inference concurrency [4, 5]. The key idea is to separate routing (gat-ing) parameters from representation (prediction) parameters , so that the gate can be adapted online while inference stability is preserved, or weights can be selectively up-dated only within the inactive subspace [4, 5, 6, 7]. We mathematically formalize sufficient conditions for concurrency and show that even under asynchronous or partial updates, the inference output at each time step can always be interpreted as a forward computation of a valid model snapshot [8, 9, 10]. This suggests that DynamicGate-MLP can serve as a practical foundation for online-adaptive and on-device learning systems [11, 12]. 

# 1 Introduction 

Modern neural networks are typically deployed under a train–then–infer paradigm [1, 13]. When training begins, inference is stopped; when inference is performed, parameters are fixed [1]. This separation is not merely a simple system design choice but inevitable , because in dense models, if parameters are continuously updated, the inference function becomes non-stationary and is not mathematically well-defined [14, 2, 3]. However, many real systems—such as edge AI, continual-learning agents, adaptive control, and personalized models—must perform inference while learning at the same time [15, 16]. The limitation that standard architectures cannot provide such concurrency calls for a new structural design [15, 17]. DynamicGate-MLP activates only a subset of neurons/blocks for each input via input-dependent gating and conditional computation [4, 5, 18]. This structure implies not only reduced compu-tation, but more fundamentally a crucial property that can make learning–inference concur-rency mathematically valid [4, 5]. This paper formalizes why concurrency is possible in DynamicGate-MLP and presents sufficient conditions and proofs [2, 3]. 1

# 2 Related Work 

Research on coping with distribution shifts (e.g., domain shift, drift) at inference time has largely evolved along two lines: Test-Time Adaptation (TTA) and Test-Time Training (TTT) .These approaches typically assume a model trained offline and then deployed, but aim to recover performance by updating a subset of parameters without labels (or with weak signals) at test time, or by gradually adapting in an input stream. However, most existing methods adjust parameters while keeping the model’s computational structure (active paths) fixed ; approaches that explicitly incorporate structural sparse execution —i.e., reducing computation (FLOPs) in an input-dependent manner—are relatively rare. 

2.1 Normalization-Based Test-Time Adaptation 

The simplest form of partial adaptation relies on re-estimating normalization statistics (e.g., BatchNorm) under the target distribution. AdaBN adapts to domain shifts by replacing Batch-Norm statistics with those estimated from the target domain [19]. TENT performs test-time adaptation by minimizing predictive entropy, while restricting updates primarily to BatchNorm-related parameters (statistics and affine terms) to improve stability and efficiency [12]. TTN points out that BatchNorm statistics can become unreliable under practical conditions such as small batch sizes and non-i.i.d. test streams, and proposes a stabilized adaptation strategy by interpolating between training-time and test-time statistics [20]. While these normalization-based methods are easy to implement and clearly realize “partial updates,” they fundamentally keep the active computational paths fixed and focus on increasing prediction confidence rather than structurally controlling computational efficiency. 

2.2 Stabilizing Entropy-Minimization TTA 

With the widespread adoption of entropy-minimization TTA, many studies have sought to mit-igate collapse, error accumulation, and forgetting during online adaptation. EATA introduces selective updates by choosing informative samples and adding regularization to preserve impor-tant parameters, thereby reducing degradation in streaming settings [21]. SAR aims to improve stability in “wild” environments by filtering unreliable samples and incorporating a sharpness-aware perspective into entropy-based adaptation [22]. CoTTA addresses continual shifts in test streams by combining prediction averaging (e.g., augmentation/weight averaging) with partial restoration mechanisms to suppress long-term forgetting [23]. Although these methods provide practical stabilization mechanisms for sustained test-time updates, they still assume a fixed com-putational graph and do not directly model or control structural variability such as fluctuating routing decisions. 

2.3 Test-Time Training: Self-Supervised Sample/Stream Adaptation 

TTT methods convert test inputs into self-supervised learning tasks and perform short updates at the sample or stream level before making predictions. TTT demonstrates that self-supervised updates at test time can improve generalization under distribution shifts [24]. TTT-MAE strengthens one-sample adaptation by using reconstruction objectives based on masked autoen-coders as the self-supervised signal [25]. MEMO adapts at test time by leveraging multiple augmentations and reducing the entropy of the predictive distribution [26]. A limitation of this family is the additional optimization and augmentation cost incurred at inference, which 2motivates careful control of update scope and computational overhead in edge or real-time deployments. 

2.4 Non-Global Updates and Source-Free Settings 

Beyond full gradient-based updates, another line of work adjusts classifiers or representations at test time. T3A proposes a lightweight, model-agnostic procedure to adjust the classifier using templates/prototypes under domain shifts [27]. SHOT studies a source-free setting where only target data are available, combining information maximization with pseudo-labeling to adapt the feature extractor while transferring the source hypothesis [28]. Although these methods dif-fer in the update target and mechanism, they commonly keep the model’s active computational paths fixed and focus on adapting representations and/or classifiers. 

2.5 Inference-Time Adaptation for Language/Foundation Models and PEFT 

Inference-time adaptation has also been explored for language models and foundation models. Dynamic Evaluation continuously updates model parameters at test time to better match recent context, improving sequence prediction performance [29]. More recently, parameter-efficient test-time adaptation has gained traction, where only small modules such as prompts, adapters, or LoRA parameters are updated instead of the full model [30, 31, 32]. While this trend makes “partial learning” more practical by reducing the number of updated parameters, it typically targets adaptation efficiency primarily through parameter count, and is conceptually distinct from approaches that dynamically reduce inference computation via input-dependent activation paths. 

2.6 Our Position: Combining Structural Plasticity with Partial Learning 

This work follows the test-time “partial update” philosophy of prior TTA/TTT methods, but differs in that adaptation is not confined to normalization, prompts, or lightweight adapters. Instead, we introduce input-dependent gating (routing) to determine active paths and enable 

structural sparse execution (conditional computation) . Moreover, we explicitly separate online updates into gate-parameter updates (𝜃 -only ), partial path-weight updates (W-only ), or both ,allowing systematic control of cost and stability. We further quantify structural fluctuations during adaptation using routing-variability metrics (e.g., flip-rate), and analyze the trade-off among stability, accuracy, and efficiency. Overall, we extend “inference-time partial learning” beyond parameter fine-tuning to an edge/streaming-friendly framework that integrates struc-tural (path-level) plasticity .

5.1 Model definition 

DynamicGate-MLP decomposes computation into routing and representation [4, 5]. Let input 𝑥 ∈ ℝ 𝑛 , weight matrix 𝑊 ∈ ℝ 𝑑×ℎ , and gate-network parameters 𝜃 . The gate function generates an input-dependent mask [4, 5]: 

𝑚(𝑥; 𝜃) ∈ {0, 1} 𝑑 (or [0, 1] 𝑑 ). (3) The forward computation is defined as [4, 5]: ̂𝑦 = 𝑓(𝑥; 𝑊 , 𝜃) = 𝐹(𝑥; 𝑊 ⊙ 𝑚(𝑥; 𝜃)), (4) where ⊙ denotes element-wise multiplication (or an equivalent structural masking operation) [7, 35]. 

5.2 Parameter decomposition 

We divide parameters into two sets [4, 5]: • Routing (=gating) parameters: 𝜃 ,• Representation parameters: 𝑊 .Importantly, not every element of 𝑊 is always used for every input [4]. If we define the active index set as 

𝑆(𝑥) = {𝑖 ∣ 𝑚 𝑖 (𝑥; 𝜃) = 1} (5) then only the subset corresponding to 𝑖 ∈ 𝑆(𝑥) contributes to the output computation [4, 5]. 

5.3 Sufficient conditions for learning–inference concurrency 

First, define “well-defined inference” as follows [2, 3]. 

Definition 1 (Well-defined inference) . Inference at time 𝑡 is said to be well-defined if there exists a parameter state (𝑊 𝑡 , 𝜃 𝑡 ) such that ̂𝑦 𝑡 = 𝑓(𝑥 𝑡 ; 𝑊 𝑡 , 𝜃 𝑡 ) (6) 

holds [2]. 

Proposition 1 (Concurrency holds under gate-only adaptation) . If 𝑊 𝑡 is fixed during infer-ence and learning updates only 𝜃 , then learning and inference can proceed concurrently without violating well-defined inference [2, 3]. 

5.4 Proof (gate-only adaptation) 

Assume: 

𝑊 𝑡+1 = 𝑊 𝑡 , (7) 5𝜃 𝑡+1 = 𝜃 𝑡 − 𝜂∇ 𝜃 ℒ(𝑊 𝑡 , 𝜃 𝑡 ), (8) where ℒ(𝑊 , 𝜃) may be the form (expected risk + regularization term) [33, 1]. For example, 

ℒ(𝑊 , 𝜃) = 𝔼 (𝑥,𝑦) [ℓ(𝑓(𝑥; 𝑊 , 𝜃), 𝑦)] + 𝜆 Ω(𝑚(𝑥; 𝜃)). (9) The inference output at time 𝑡 is ̂𝑦 𝑡 = 𝑓(𝑥 𝑡 ; 𝑊 𝑡 , 𝜃 𝑡 ) (10) and the update of 𝜃 is performed after this output is computed [2]. Therefore, ̂ 𝑦 𝑡 is an exact forward computation of the fixed model snapshot (𝑊 𝑡 , 𝜃 𝑡 ) [2, 3]. For the next input 𝑥 𝑡+1 ,

(𝑊 𝑡 , 𝜃 𝑡+1 ) serves as a new snapshot that again defines a valid model [2]. Thus, at every time step, inference is always well-defined by some snapshot model [2]. 

5.5 Selective weight updates on the active subspace 

DynamicGate-MLP permits an even stronger form of concurrency [34, 6]. Suppose we selectively update only the active subspace [7, 6]: 

𝑊 (𝑖) 𝑡+1 = { 𝑊 (𝑖) 𝑡 − 𝜂 ∇ 𝑊 (𝑖) ℓ(𝑓(𝑥 𝑡 ; 𝑊 𝑡 , 𝜃 𝑡 ), 𝑦 𝑡 ), 𝑖 ∈ 𝑆(𝑥 𝑡 ), 𝑊 (𝑖) 𝑡 , 𝑖 ∉ 𝑆(𝑥 𝑡 ). (11) Observe that if 𝑖 ∉ 𝑆(𝑥 𝑡 ), then 𝑚 𝑖 (𝑥 𝑡 ; 𝜃 𝑡 ) = 0 , so 𝑊 (𝑖) does not contribute to 𝑓(𝑥 𝑡 ; 𝑊 𝑡 , 𝜃 𝑡 ) [4, 5]. Therefore, even if the update is restricted to 𝑖 ∈ 𝑆(𝑥 𝑡 ), the definition of the inference output at time 𝑡 is not corrupted [7, 6]. 

Proposition 2 (Concurrency also holds under active-subspace updates) . If updates are re-stricted to the active subspace induced by the gate, then learning and inference can proceed concurrently while preserving well-defined inference [34, 6]. 

5.6 Interpretation: piecewise-stationary model 

DynamicGate-MLP forms a sequence of snapshots over time [2, 9]: 

(𝑊 𝑡 , 𝜃 𝑡 ) → (𝑊 𝑡+1 , 𝜃 𝑡+1 ). (12) Inference at each time can be interpreted as a stationary model at the corresponding snapshot [2]. This is fundamentally different from dense models in which all parameters affect all inputs [4, 5]. 

5.7 Asynchronous and practical concurrency 

In real systems, parameter copies for serving and training can be separated [10, 9, 8]: • Serving parameters (𝑊 𝑠 , 𝜃 𝑠 ),• Training parameters (𝑊 𝑡 , 𝜃 𝑡 ).Inference proceeds as ̂𝑦 = 𝑓(𝑥; 𝑊 𝑠 , 𝜃 𝑠 ), (13) and learning proceeds as 

(𝑊 𝑡 , 𝜃 𝑡 ) ← (𝑊 𝑡 , 𝜃 𝑡 ) − 𝜂∇ℒ(𝑊 𝑡 , 𝜃 𝑡 ) (14) 6[10, 9, 8]. Then, with periodic synchronization (snapshot), 

(𝑊 𝑠 , 𝜃 𝑠 ) ← Snapshot (𝑊 𝑡 , 𝜃 𝑡 ) (15) the serving side always operates stably with fixed snapshot parameters [9]. Since DynamicGate-MLP often updates only a subset of parameters that are meaningfully involved per input, syn-chronization cost can be reduced [4, 5, 9]. 

# 6 Experiments 

6.1 Experimental Protocol 

This experiment quantitatively compares how online adaptation under distribution shift (data drift) affects performance, stability, and computational cost [36, 11]. After training each model offline on a clean distribution, we evaluate on a drifted data stream by (i) measuring accuracy before adaptation (DriftBefore), and (ii) performing online adaptation and then measuring drifted-distribution accuracy after adaptation (AdaptAcc) and clean-distribution performance change (CleanDrop) [36, 12, 37]. We also record the degree of prediction changes before/after adaptation (Flip), activation ratio (AR), and relative FLOPs (FLOPs~) to jointly analyze efficiency and stability [12, 17]. 

6.2 Compared Models 

• Dense: A standard MLP without sparse routing (gate/router). Since it has no 𝜃 or inactive-W concept required by online adaptation modes (A/B/C/D), those modes are skipped [1]. • DG-Hard: Hard-gating-based DynamicGate. It is efficient with low AR/FLOPs, but due to the non-differentiability of hard selection, 𝜃 -only (B) updates may be implementation-limited [4, 18]. • DG-Soft: Soft-gating-based DynamicGate. Flexible routing yields large drift recovery (Recovery), but may increase Flip and CleanDrop [12, 17]. • DG-Anneal: A DG variant that gradually sharpens routing from soft to hard (annealing). It aims for a performance–stability compromise [18]. • MoE-Top1: Top-1 hard-routing MoE. It is efficient with low AR/FLOPs, and in this setup online adaptation is performed only in mode D [5, 18]. • MoE-Soft: Soft-routing MoE. All experts are partially active (AR ≈1), so computational cost is high, but final AdaptAcc tends to be high [5, 38, 39]. 

6.3 Online Adaptation Modes (A/B/C/D) 

• A (None): No parameter update during the online stage (baseline) [2]. • B ( 𝜃 -only): Update only routing/gating parameters 𝜃 , with W frozen. Fast, but may increase Flip and risk CleanDrop (forgetting) [12, 17]. • C (Inactive-W only): Update only representation weights W with low contribution (in-active) for the current input, with 𝜃 frozen. Aims for stability and clean-performance preservation [17, 15]. 7• D ( 𝜃 + Inactive-W): Update 𝜃 and inactive W together. It generally provides the highest AdaptAcc, but stability depends on the model/routing type [12, 15]. 

6.4 Evaluation Metrics 

• DriftBefore: Drifted-distribution accuracy before online adaptation (%) [36, 11]. • AdaptAcc: Drifted-distribution accuracy after online adaptation (%) [12, 37]. • Recovery: AdaptAcc - DriftBefore (%p). Accuracy gain recovered under drift [11]. • CleanDrop: Change in clean-distribution accuracy after adaptation (%p). Closer to 0 indicates less forgetting [17, 16]. • Flip: Ratio of samples whose predicted class changes before/after adaptation (0~1). Larger values may indicate instability/forgetting [12, 17]. • AR: Average activation ratio (ratio activated by gate/router) [4, 5, 18]. • FLOPs: Relative computation cost (1.00 = full computation) [4, 18, 35]. • 𝜃 /W params: Number of parameters updated during online adaptation [4, 5].

6.6 Summary of Experimental Results 

The results show that (i) joint adaptation of routing ( 𝜃 ) and representation weights (W) is effec-tive for drift recovery, and (ii) soft routing provides high adaptation performance but can accom-pany prediction flips (Flip) and degradation on clean data (CleanDrop) [12, 37, 17]. Therefore, in real-time/edge scenarios, hard-routing (Top1/Hard)-based low-cost adaptation may be advan-tageous, whereas when soft routing is used, stabilization mechanisms (e.g., routing-change regu-larization, conservative 𝜃 updates, annealing) are needed to control Flip and CleanDrop [18, 5].

The results in Table 2 show the following characteristics from the perspectives of (i) vulner-ability to drift , (ii) online adaptation gain , (iii) stability (Flip) , and (iv) efficiency (FLOPs~, AR) .

(1) Vulnerability to drift: sharp drop in DriftAcc All models have high CleanAcc around 94.8–96.9, but DriftAcc drops sharply to 22.4–39.7. This means that high performance on the clean distribution does not directly translate into robustness under distribution shift, and suggests that drift handling (adaptation or robustness enhancement) is necessary in real streaming/field environments. 

(2) Online adaptation gain: degree of recovery in AdaptAcc The recovery in post-adaptation performance (AdaptAcc) varies greatly by model. • Dense changes only marginally from DriftAcc 28.48 to AdaptAcc 28.56. This shows that without routing/conditional computation, the “adaptation” setting in this experiment does not translate into drift recovery. • DG-Hard rises slightly from DriftAcc 33.97 to AdaptAcc 34.21. In other words, even with an efficient structure, the adaptation gain may be limited. • DG-Soft rises sharply from DriftAcc 22.42 to AdaptAcc 74.00. (3) Stability (Flip(drift)) tradeoff Flip(drift) indicates how often predictions flip in the drift segment (a signal of instability/volatility). • DG-Hard has Flip(drift)=0.104, which is very low and thus highly stable. In contrast, its adaptation gain (AdaptAcc increase) is limited. • DG-Soft has Flip(drift)=0.953, and DG-Anneal has 0.996, both very high. Thus, large AdaptAcc recovery is accompanied by very high prediction volatility .• MoE-Soft has Flip(drift)=0.429, lower than DG-Soft/DG-Anneal, and MoE-Top1 is at an intermediate level with 0.615. Therefore, the results show that strong adaptation recovery (high AdaptAcc) can appear together with high prediction flipping (Flip) , and when reliability/stability is important in deployment, controlling Flip becomes a key design factor. 13 (4) Relationship between efficiency (AR, FLOPs~) and performance AR(drift) and FLOPs~ represent the efficiency of conditional-computation structures. • MoE-Top1 is the most efficient with AR=0.250 and FLOPs~=0.25, but its AdaptAcc is 37.48, indicating limited recovery. • DG-Hard is also efficient with AR=0.297 and FLOPs~=0.30, and has low Flip. Thus it is strong in terms of efficiency + stability .• DG-Soft/DG-Anneal have FLOPs~=0.91, close to full computation, and high AR values of 0.908/0.913. They obtain strong AdaptAcc recovery (74.00, 61.60), but pay high costs in efficiency (compute) and stability (Flip). • Dense and MoE-Soft have AR=1.000 and FLOPs~=1.00 (full computation), and with equal or larger computation than DG-Soft/DG-Anneal, their AdaptAcc values are 28.56 and 44.68, respectively. Overall, the results suggest that (i) high-efficiency (low FLOPs~) models may be stable but may have limited adaptation gain , and (ii) strong adaptation recovery can be coupled with high AR and high Flip . Thus, in practical applications, the design choice may diverge depending on the objective: (A) stability-oriented (DG-Hard, MoE-Soft) or (B) adaptation-performance-oriented (DG-Soft, DG-Anneal). 

(5) Practical implications 

• Stability first (e.g., medical/safety/policy): Structures with low Flip (e.g., DG-Hard) are favorable, and adaptation should be applied conservatively (learning rate/frequency limits) or with snapshot/validation stages. • Adaptation performance first (e.g., personalization/rapidly changing environ-ments): Large AdaptAcc recovery in DG-Soft is attractive, but Flip(drift) can become very large, so prediction-volatility mitigation (e.g., calibration, regularization, update lim-its) is needed. • Edge efficiency first (battery/latency): Low-FLOPs~ models such as MoE-Top1 are advantageous, but additional adaptation design is needed for drift recovery.

# 9 Discussion 

9.1 Why this is not a “hack” 

The serving output is always defined by some valid parameter snapshot [2, 9]. In addition, if snapshots are fixed at the serving boundary, partially updated parameters do not get mixed 16 into the output of a single request [9, 8]. This is compatible with standard definitions of online learning and deployed inference [2, 3]. 

9.2 Biological/neuromorphic perspective 

The intuition that synaptic strength changes relatively slowly while active pathways or attention adapt quickly is analogous to the routing–representation decomposition in this structure. 

9.3 Hardware/system perspective 

DynamicGate-MLP naturally aligns with block-sparse accelerators, enables online adaptation without full-model retraining, and provides a practical path toward on-device continual learn-ing [35, 18]. 

# 10 Conclusion 

This paper showed that DynamicGate-MLP permits learning–inference concurrency through the structural separation of routing and representation parameters [4, 5]. Through sufficient conditions and proofs, we established that even under asynchronous or partial updates, the inference output at each time step can be interpreted as a forward computation of a valid snapshot model [10, 9, 8]. This provides a foundation for adaptive neural systems that can overcome the concurrency limitations of conventional dense models [36, 11, 15].
