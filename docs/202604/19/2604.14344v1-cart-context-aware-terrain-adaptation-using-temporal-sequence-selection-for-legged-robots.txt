# CART: Context-Aware Terrain Adaptation using Temporal Sequence Selection for Legged Robots 

Kartikeya Singh, Youngjin Kim, Yash Turkar, and Karthik Dantu University at Buffalo 

Abstract — Animals in nature combine multiple modalities, such as sight and feel, to perceive terrain and develop an understanding of how to walk on uneven terrain in a stable manner. Similarly, legged robots need to develop their ability to stably walk on complex terrains by developing an under-standing of the relationship between vision and proprioception. Most current terrain adaptation methods are susceptible to failure on complex, off-road terrain as they rely on prior experience, particularly observations from a vision sensor. This experience-based learning often creates a Visual-Texture Paradox between what has been seen and how it actually feels. In this work, we introduce CART, a high-level controller built on a context-aware terrain adaptation approach that integrates proprioception and exteroception from onboard sensing to achieve a robust understanding of terrain. We evaluate our method on multiple terrains using an ANYmal-C robot on the IsaacSim simulator and a Boston Dynamics SPOT robot for our real-world experiments. To evaluate the learned contextual terrain properties, we adapt vibrational stability on the base of the robot as a metric. We compare CART with various state-of-the-art baselines equipped with multimodal sensing in both simulation and the real world. CART achieves an average success rate improvement of 5% over all baselines in simulation and improves the overall stability up to 45% and 24% in the real world without increasing the time taken by the robot to accomplish locomotion tasks. 

I. INTRODUCTION Autonomous navigation of legged robots through diverse and complicated environments has gained attention over the past few years to perform critical tasks such as payload delivery [1], search and rescue [2], environmental inspec-tion [3], precision agriculture [4], and others. Legged robots such as Boston Dynamics’ SPOT [5], Unitree Go1/Go2 [6], and Anybotics ANYmal [7] provide basic legged locomotion, enabling them to walk on different terrains. The primary challenge in using them for off-road navigation is to in-corporate a detailed understanding of the terrain and use this for high-level planning and control for safe and stable locomotion. Several previous approaches identify rich semantic infor-mation of an off-road terrain using images [8], [9], [10], [11]. These methods are capable of identifying semantic class distributions across distinct off-road environments but are not robust to illumination and viewpoint changes. To overcome these issues, other works make use of LiDAR point clouds [12], [13] that remain unperturbed in varying lighting conditions. Additionally, diffusion using various high-level sensors [14], [15] results in a more detailed representation

These exteroception-based approaches are robust to the segmentations of fine-grained terrains but do not account for the control/planning and stable locomotion of a robot (specifically, legged robots) on these segmented classes. Sim-to-real approaches [16], [17] have been explored in establishing twin policies to make the robot learn Appear-ance and Tactility about the terrain type explicitly. However, these approaches are tightly coupled with the simulated representations they acquire for training (such as scan-dots in [16] and privileged scanning in [18]). These simulated experience-based learning approaches are well crafted to adapt the physical attributes such as agility, traction control, or dexterity to perform control and coordination tasks, but lack adaptation to real-world nuances such as the Visual-Texture Paradox . This discrepancy between visual and sen-sory artifacts could lead to overly conservative and further unstable locomotion. In this work, we present a unified learn-ing paradigm for legged robots that integrates exteroceptive and proprioceptive sensory modalities to enhance contextual understanding between visual perception and physical inter-action. We further utilize the contexts established by CART to develop a high-level control policy prioritizing the robot’s stability on complex terrains. Some prior works [20], [21], [22] have relied solely on exteroception to plan and navigate legged robots through complex environments. However, methods using propriocep-tion (velocity/hip positions) from the robot’s locomotion, along with exteroception from distinct perception sensors (camera/lidar), have also been used for terrain adaptation [23], [24], [16], depicting the stable locomotion robustness. These approaches not only estimate the terrain representation but also adapt to stable locomotion by using proprioceptive signals like hip positions and efforts, highlighting the crucial role of proprioception in maintaining robot stability [23]. We build CART using the same motivation while using proprioceptive signals as the reward function to account for vibrational stability. A more detailed related survey of our adapted stability metric is mentioned in subsection II-B. One of the instances depicting a visual-texture paradox scenario has been shown in Figure 1. Our primary obser-vation is that the stability of the robot is related to the nature of the terrain model is learning, which can only be determined by establishing a context between the visual appearance of the terrain and how it is physically felt by the robot when it traverses that terrain. A. Terrain Estimation Using Sensing 

Legged robots’ navigation capability has been vali-dated [26], [16] across complex, uneven, and varying terrain while ensuring safety and efficiency. With the hardware advancements in the industry, these robots have not been left untouched from performing some of the extreme navigation tasks like [27], [17]. Perpetual ob-servation of terrain is a vital step in making robust and collision-free navigation of a robot successful. Sensor-based perception using exteroception has been exploited over the years, which could result in a high-level but coarse characterization of a specific terrain. Leveraging sensor-based perception works like [9], and [28] utilizes images to determine risk-aware path planning for a legged robot. However, these methods, without any additional signal, result in a lack of internal feedback and could make the robot’s body unaware of its current state. To overcome these issues, [23] couples both exteroceptive and proprioceptive parameters of a robot to adapt between different gaits. Similarly, RL-based methods like [29], [16] generalize over different types of legged robots by adapting the various privileged information like simulated terrain profile, friction coefficients, and robot proprio-ception, but they are not well acquainted with the real-world terrain representations because of the sim-to-real distinction. 

B. Vibration damping for sensors on robotic platforms 

Legged robots like Boston Dynamics’ Spot and ANYMAL-C are designed to traverse complex, uneven terrain. However, these highly actuated robots are susceptible to creating intense, high-frequency motion jitteriness at the robot’s base (pitch, roll, yaw). Tightly coupled sensors like camera/LiDAR inherit vibrations occurring on the base of the robot, leading to severe image blur or point cloud distortion [30], inefficient localization and mapping [31][32] and descriptor matching [33]. An entire domain of research in image and video stabilization is dedicated to addressing these issues. [34] presents a stabilized vision system by using a fast pan and tilt unit (PTU) on the fully torque-controlled hydraulically actuated quadruped robot (HyQ) to compensate for the vibrations, impacts, or slippages on rough terrain. On the other hand, [35] tackles the problem of SLAM degradation due to vibrations in the coupled sensors in rugged terrains by proposing a hybrid gimbal system with virtual suspension control and terrain-adaptive planning. Therefore, our work stabilizes the vibrations on the robot’s base to compensate for vibrations occurring in the coupled sensors, like cameras/LiDAR mounted on the robot.

However, the constrained range of motion and non-adaptability in the robot’s state bring suboptimal outcomes in robust-legged locomotion. A. Overview and Notion of Stability 

CART consists of two components: (i) a context-aware high-level command policy and (ii) a Temporal Sequence Selection (TSS) module (Fig. 2). CART outputs base-level 

commands that are executed by an underlying low-level locomotion controller (Spot’s built-in controller on hard-ware and a learned low-level controller in simulation). The low-level controller is treated as a black box that converts commanded base motion into joint-level actuation. 

Vibrational stability. In this work, “stability” refers to 

vibrational stability on the robot base: reducing oscilla-tory disturbances induced by foot–ground interaction that propagate to the base and can degrade onboard sensing (camera/depth) and perception-action coupling. This is dis-tinct from whole-body locomotion stability in the classical sense. 

B. Multimodal Observations and Context Fusion 

At each timestep t, the robot observes: St = [ Sv,t , Sm,t , Sp,t ],

where Sv,t denotes RGB-D observations, Sm,t denotes terrain geometry features, and Sp,t denotes proprioceptive feedback. 

1) Visual Encoding: We encode the RGB-D input 

IRGBD ,t ∈ R4×360 ×640 using a convolutional encoder: zv,t =

fv(IRGBD ,t ) ∈ R256 . In our architecture, fv(·) consists of three convolution layers with ReLU activations, followed by adaptive average pooling, flattening, and a linear pro-jection to 256 dimensions. 2) Mesh Encoding: We encode mesh-based terrain geom-etry Xmesh ,t ∈ R128 via a fully-connected encoder: zm,t =

fm(Xmesh ,t ) ∈ R256 . The encoder is implemented as a two-layer MLP. 

3) Proprioceptive Encoding: We encode proprioception using a bidirectional LSTM: zp,t = fp(Sp,t ) ∈ R256 .

4) Attention-Based Context Vector: To explicitly model the relationship between terrain appearance/geometry and physical interaction feedback (thereby addressing the visual–texture paradox), we fuse exteroceptive latents us-ing an attention mechanism: ct = Attn (zv,t , zm,t ) ∈ R256 .

In our implementation, Attn (·) is realized via multi-head attention applied to the pair (zv,t , zm,t ), followed by aggregation. The final context-aware representation is formed by concatenating the fused exteroceptive context with the proprioceptive embedding: ˆ St = [ ct ; zp,t ] ∈ R512 .

This compact representation is used both for high-level command generation and for TSS context scoring. 

C. Hierarchical Execution: High-Level Outputs vs Low-Level Controller 

A key design choice in CART is compatibility with off-the-shelf legged platforms whose joint-level controllers are proprietary or otherwise inaccessible. CART therefore operates in a hierarchical manner: 

High-level (CART) outputs. CART outputs base-level commands: 

at = [ vx, vy, vz, h], (1) where (vx, vy, vz) are commanded base linear velocities and 

h is commanded body height. 

Low-level execution. The underlying low-level locomo-tion controller consumes (vx, vy, vz, h) and produces joint-level actuation (e.g., desired joint positions/torques) to track the commanded base motion and height. On Spot, this mapping is performed internally by the built-in con-troller; in simulation, the same interface is provided by a learned low-level controller. CART is therefore controller-agnostic: it can be deployed on different robots and low-level controllers as long as a standard base-command interface is available.

B. Real-world Experiment Setup 

Our real-world experiments were conducted using aBoston Dynamics’ Spot robot on various rugged terrains with varying elevations as shown in Figure 5. We follow the same point-to-point navigation task schema as the simulation. We compare the performance of CART with Spot’s against the following baselines: 

Spot’s Inbuilt (p+e) [5]: The internal feedback system of Spot is equipped with an advanced stability mechanism to monitor and regulate the acceleration and stability of the robot. The real-time feedback loop from the robot’s internal software helps it to adjust the 3d acceleration even during a manual input of velocities. In our evaluations, we compare CART with three different walking modes from the spot’s inbuilt controller: TROT , CRAWL , and 

AMBLE using identical commanded velocities. 

VAPOR (p+e) [24]: Risk-aware stable navigation method that computes the robot’s velocity using exeroception and proprioception to maintain a stable locomotion in real-world unstructured environments. VAPOR provides optimal robot candidate actions and velocity commands using its proposed stability vector. We utilize their state outputs from their planner and compare the stability performance against CART. To ensure consistency in our evaluation experiments, identical average speeds were used to evaluate the stability performance of the robot using various walking styles (TROT, CRAWL, and AM-BLE) and CART. Additionally, we evaluate a terrain-aware stability improvement model [24] using its velocity space restriction module, designed for real-world environments, against CART using the same identical average velocities. We compare CART with various baselines in the real-world setting by adapting 7 different off-road scenarios as shown in Figure 5. We experienced a lack of publicly available baselines that are agnostic to the legged robot, as they remain highly distinct in their proprioceptive parameters, as shown in [47]. Re-implementing the baselines from simulation to the real world requires proprietary software kits such as [48] that could be complex and expensive. There-fore, we compare CART against robot-specific locomotion baselines for simulation and real-world.

We present CART, a context-aware terrain adaptation method for robust and stable navigation for quadruped robots in irregular and complex terrains. Our approach incorporates the robot’s exteroception and proprioception to estimate the desired velocity and height. Our approach not only accounts for the stability of the robot while navigating on irregular ter-rains but also adapts to real-world nuances such as the visual-texture paradox to accurately predict the pseudo-terrain prop-erties. Our learning framework incorporates the visual rep-resentations of specific terrain and creates a correspondence with the appropriate velocity and height required for specific terrain. We evaluate our method in terms of the notion of the robot’s stability and simulation-based success rates with an improvement up to 5%. We observe our method to acquire more stability up to an average of 45 % in simulation and 

24 % in the real world when tested on various unseen irreg-ular terrains with varying elevations. The modular approach presented in this work is not limited to predicting high-level states suitable for the robot to traverse complex terrains. The Temporal Sequence Selection Framework (TSS) module presented in this work provides the flexibility to unwrap many other parameters acquired by the robot while training, such as low-level torque, body orientation, COM projection, etc. These parameters could be utilized for adaptive path planning, semantics-based reversibility analysis for mobile robots, and energy-aware navigation.
