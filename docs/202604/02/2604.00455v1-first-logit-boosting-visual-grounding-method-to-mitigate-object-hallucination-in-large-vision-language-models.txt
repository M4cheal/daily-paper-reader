## Abstract 

Recent Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance across various mul-timodal tasks that require understanding both visual and linguistic inputs. However, object hallucination — the gen-eration of nonexistent objects in answers — remains a per-sistent challenge. Although several approaches such as re-training and external grounding methods have been pro-posed to mitigate this issue, they still suffer from high data costs or structural complexity. Training-free meth-ods such as Contrastive Decoding (CD) are more cost-effective, avoiding additional training or external models, but still suffer from long-term decay, where visual ground-ing weakens and language priors dominate as the gen-eration progresses. In this paper, we propose First Logit Boosting (FLB), a simple yet effective training-free tech-nique designed to alleviate long-term decay in LVLMs. FLB stores the logit of the first generated token and adds it to subsequent token predictions, effectively mitigating long-term decay of visual information. We observe that FLB (1) sustains the visual information embedded in the first to-ken throughout generation, and (2) suppresses hallucinated words through the stabilizing effect of the “The” token. Ex-perimental results show that FLB significantly reduces ob-ject hallucination across various tasks, benchmarks, and backbone models. Notably, it causes negligible inference overhead, making it highly applicable to real-time multi-modal systems. Code is available at https://github. com/jiwooha20/FLB

Hence, addressing object hallucination remains essential for advancing LVLMs toward reliable real-world deployment. To mitigate this issue, existing studies can be broadly categorized into three lines of research. (1) Retraining-based methods mitigate hallucination through additional training, such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) [10, 28, 40] on large-scale annotated datasets, or by architectural modifications such as posi-tional embedding adjustments [36]. (2) External ground-ing methods reduce hallucination by additional models that verify the existence of generated objects or supplement the

missing ones [6, 24, 38]. (3) Training-free methods in-tervene directly in the logit or latent space at inference time, aiming to suppress hallucination without additional data or model components [8, 13, 15, 22, 33, 34, 39, 42]. Nevertheless, all three lines exhibit inherent trade-offs: re-training methods are data-intensive and cost-intensive, ex-ternal ground methods compromise efficiency, and exist-ing training-free methods fail to sustain visual grounding over long generations, leading to persistent hallucinations. Among the three methods, training-free approaches have recently gained attention for their efficiency and simplic-ity, as they operate entirely during the inference time with-out additional supervision or model retraining. Contrastive Decoding (CD), a representative training-free method, sup-presses hallucination by contrasting the logits from the orig-inal and perturbed inputs, thereby reducing over-reliance on language priors and statistical biases [8, 15, 22, 33, 39]. However, these methods fail to fully address a critical issue, 

long-term decay of visual grounding [8, 24]. As text genera-tion progresses, the model’s attention gradually drifts away from visual evidence and becomes dominated by language priors, causing hallucinations to frequently appear in later tokens. Moreover, CD methods require two forward passes per decoding step, one for the original input and the other for the perturbed version, which doubles inference time. To overcome these limitations, we propose a simple yet effective training-free technique, named First Logit Boost-ing (FLB) , motivated by the key observation that the first token’s logit inherently encodes the most visually grounded representation in LVLMs. Specifically, at the first decod-ing step, the relative logit gap between the ground truth and hallucination tokens is the largest. By storing the first to-ken logit and reusing it in subsequent decoding steps, FLB continuously reinforces early visual grounding throughout the entire generation process. As illustrated in Fig. 1, FLB leverages two complementary mechanisms that jointly sta-bilize decoding: (1) a direct visual grounding effect where the first token logit serves as a persistent visual anchor re-taining visual evidence, and (2) an implicit visual referenc-ing effect where the increased likelihood of sentence start-ing with “The” token encourages the model refer back to entities generated earlier before long-term decay becomes severe, thereby reducing the chance of hallucination. These dual effects effectively mitigate the long-term decay prob-lem without introducing additional training and external models. Moreover, unlike previous training-free methods such as CD, which require dual forward passes per decoding step, FLB achieves superior hallucination mitigation using only a single forward pass with negligible inference over-head. Our main contributions are as follows: • We propose FLB, a lightweight and training-free tech-nique that effectively mitigates object hallucination caused by long-term decay without requiring additional training or external modules. • We identify and analyze that FLB’s improvement stems from two complementary effects, a direct visual ground-ing effect and an implicit visual referencing effect. • Extensive experiments demonstrate that FLB achieves state-of-the-art hallucination mitigation performance across various benchmarks and backbone models, while preserving near-baseline inference efficiency. 

## 2. Related work 

2.1. Large Vision-Language Models 

LVLMs [5, 19, 31, 41] typically consist of three compo-nents: a vision encoder (e.g., CLIP [25]), a Large Language Model (LLM) (e.g., LLaMA [29], Vicuna [4]) and cross-modal alignment module such as the Q-Former in Instruct-BLIP [5] or the Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) projector in LLaVA-1.5 [19]. This architecture enables LVLMs to effec-tively integrate multimodal inputs and achieve strong per-formance on tasks like image captioning and VQA [11]. However, they still suffer from object hallucination, where generated captions mention non-existent objects [1, 20]. 

2.2. Mitigating hallucination 

Object hallucination in LVLMs arises from multiple causes, and consequently, various mitigation approaches have been explored. Existing approaches can be roughly categorized into three groups: (1) training-based methods [28, 40], (2) external grounding methods [6, 24, 38], and (3) training-free methods [13, 15, 22]. Specifically, training-free ap-proaches typically operate on the logit or latent space dur-ing decoding to suppress language priors, the tendency of LVLMs to rely more on textual context than on visual input, without additional models or retraining. Among these, CD methods mitigate hallucination by contrasting logits from the original input with those from a distorted input that am-plifies language priors. Different CD variants use distorted inputs such as a noised image [15], a perturbed instruc-tion [33], or an unconditioned input [8]. Despite their effectiveness, prior studies [24] have shown that the influence of language priors grows with sentence length, and existing CD methods fail to fully address this issue. Moreover, CD methods require additional decod-ing passes. To overcome these limitations, we propose a training-free method which eliminates additional inference passes while alleviating the reinforcement of language pri-ors that intensify with longer generations. Unlike previous logit steering methods that steer generation toward a tar-get distribution, FLB instead leverages the earliest decod-ing distribution, which is the most visually grounded, with the “The” effect reflecting stabilization from reduced uncer-tainty. 3. CD methods provide a model-agnostic and training-free way to mitigate hallucination. Addressing these two limitations motivates the design of our method introduced in the following section. 

## 4. Method 

4.1. First Logit Boosting 

To mitigate the amplification of language priors in LVLMs during long sequences, we propose a simple yet effec-tive technique named First Logit Boosting (FLB). Our key intuition originates from the positional characteristics of LVLMs: the first token is generated immediately after vi-sual tokens, before cross-modal attention begins to decay. Hence, the first token’s logit is minimally affected by the decay and therefore preserves the original visual informa-tion most faithfully. 4.2. Direct visual grounding effect 

The first effect of FLB directly addresses the long-term de-cay of visual grounding. As discussed in Sec. 3.3, LVLMs suffer from the long-term decay due to RoPE, which weak-ens cross-modal attention as the distance between image and text tokens increases. This section investigates how FLB directly addresses this issue by leveraging the logit of the first token as a persistent visual anchor. To quantita-tively examine the effect, we analyze the logit difference be-tween the ground truth and hallucination tokens contained in the first logit in Eq. (3). As shown in Fig. 3, the ground truth words exhibit consistently higher logits than the hal-lucinated ones, demonstrating that the first token retains the strongest visual correlation. As FLB reuses this logit across all subsequent decoding steps, this margin is repeatedly ap-plied, continuously reinforcing visual signals. 

4.3. “The” effect 

While FLB is originally designed to mitigate visual decay through the direct visual grounding effect, we unexpectedly observe a complementary effect arising from the linguis-tic properties of the first token. As illustrated in Fig. 4, the first decoding step generates articles such as “The”, “In”, and “A”, which subtly influence the model’s subsequent predictions. Surprisingly, sentences beginning with “The” exhibit markedly lower hallucination rates, and nouns fol-lowing “The” are more frequently grounded in visual input than those following “A” or the other starting tokens. This emergent effect, named “The” effect , reveals that linguistic initialization can act as an implicit form of visual ground-ing. Even without any modification to the model’s language prior, FLB amplifies this bias toward contextually and visu-ally grounded nouns by reusing the first token logit, leading to more coherent and visually consistent generations. We empirically validate this effect in Sec. 6.4 where we provide a detailed analysis of (i) sentences starting with “The”, (ii) nouns immediately following “The”, and (iii) the reduction in prediction entropy after “The”, jointly offering a compre-hensive account of the “The” effect .

## 5. Experiment 

In this section, we demonstrate the effectiveness of the pro-posed FLB in mitigating hallucination. Since FLB is de-signed to suppress the influence of language priors that be-come stronger as sentences grow longer, its effectiveness in mitigating hallucination increases accordingly. Therefore, we focused on generative tasks, where such effects are more evident, rather than discriminative ones. 

5.1. Datasets and evaluation metrics 

We conducted main experiments on two standard bench-marks that require long and descriptive caption generation: the CHAIR [26] and AMBER [30] datasets. For CHAIR, we used 500 images randomly sampled from the MSCOCO 2014 validation set [18]. For the AMBER benchmark, we used all 1,004 images from the AMBER dataset. Cap-tions were generated using the prompt on both benchmarks: 

“Please describe this image in detail.” The generated cap-tions were then evaluated using following metrics [26, 30]: (1) CHAIR (CHAIR i), the proportion of hallucinated ob-jects among all generated objects; (2) Cover , the proportion of ground truth objects among all generated objects; (3) Hal 

(CHAIR s), the proportion of captions containing at least one hallucinated object; and (4) Cog , the proportion of halluci-nated objects that are perceptually plausible to humans. To further evaluate the generalization capability of FLB beyond captioning tasks, we additionally conducted exper-iments on diverse tasks and interaction styles. We used two benchmarks: MMHalBench [28], which includes di-verse question types (e.g., object attributes, relations, and contextual reasoning) beyond image captioning, and Con-vBench [21], a three-turn conversation-style benchmark. Both benchmarks are evaluated using GPT-4V as the judge. 

5.2. MLLM backbones 

To verify the generality of FLB, we evaluated the two benchmarks on LVLMs employing different types of vi-sual–language alignment. Specifically, we used two repre-sentative models: LLaVA-1.5 (7B) [19], which connects vi-sion and language modalities through an MLP-based pro-jection layer, and InstructBLIP (7B) [5], which adopts a Q-former [16] architecture for cross-modal alignment. 

5.3. Baselines 

To compare FLB with existing training-free hallucina-tion mitigation methods, we included Baseline decod-ing (vanilla decoding without any hallucination mitigation method) and three representative contrastive decoding tech-niques as baselines: VCD [15], ICD [33], and M3ID [8]. VCD alleviates hallucination by contrasting logits obtained from an original image and a distorted image, while ICD performs contrastive decoding using an original instruction and a perturbed instruction. M3ID reduce hallucinations by minimizing visual information loss using image condi-tioned input and unconditioned input. All experiments were conducted with random sampling-based decoding. Each re-sult was averaged over three runs to ensure consistency. For each baseline, we adopted hyperparameter configurations that achieved the best performance as reported in the orig-inal papers [8, 15, 33]. The hyperparameters of FLB were set to γ = 0 .3, λ = 0 .05 , and β = 0 .1, as detailed in Sec. B.

## 7. Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduced FLB, a lightweight and training-free decoding method that mitigates long-term ob-ject hallucination in LVLMs by reusing the first-token logit throughout generation. Unlike prior approaches that intro-duce additional models or require double inference, FLB achieves hallucination mitigation through reusing only the first token logit. FLB leverages two complementary mech-anisms, direct visual grounding and implicit visual refer-encing, and achieves consistent improvements in various LVLMs and hallucination benchmarks. 

Limitations and future directions. Although effective, FLB does not fundamentally resolve the structural limita-tions of training-free decoding. In particular, FLB cannot fully model context-dependent visual grounding as it in-jects visual evidence through the first-token logit rather than adapting to evolving visual semantics. Furthermore, it does not eliminate the underlying long-term decay introduced by RoPE, but instead mitigates its effect through early-token reinforcement. Extending FLB with adaptive token-level visual grounding or decay-aware positional mecha-nisms would be interesting future directions. Acknowledgements

B.2. Effect of β in adaptive plausible constraint 

To understand how the magnitude of adaptive plausible con-straint affects the performance of FLB, we conducted a hy-perparameter optimization over β. Tab. 15 shows the results of the hyperparameter optimization for β. A larger value of 

β leads to more aggressive truncation, allowing only tokens with higher probabilities to be selected. We conducted this optimization on LLaVA-1.5 using the AMBER benchmark. As shown in Tab. 15, both excessively large and excessively small values of β limit the performance improvement of FLB. This indicates that overly aggressive or insufficient truncation can negatively affect the model’s ability to sup-press hallucinations while preserving correct information. In addition, when the adaptive plausible constraint is not applied (i.e., β = 0 ), we observed unnatural token selec-tions, such as spurious insertions of tokens like “The” in the middle of a sentence, as illustrated in Fig. 9. When the con-straint is applied (e.g., β = 0 .1), this erroneous behavior disappears, suggesting that the adaptive plausible constraint plays an important role in stabilizing token selection. Based on these results, we selected 0.1 as the value of β, which achieves the highest object score and provides the best bal-ance between reducing hallucinations and maintaining cor-rect object coverage. 

B.3. Effect of decoding strategy 

In addition to the sampling-based decoding used in our main experiments, we also evaluated the performance under greedy decoding, where the token with the highest proba-bility is selected at each step. As shown in Tab. 16, FLB consistently mitigates hallucination even when the decoding strategy is switched to greedy decoding, demonstrating that its effectiveness is robust to the choice of decoding method. However, we also observed a slight decrease in Cover score under greedy decoding. This suggests that the deter-ministic nature of greedy decoding may restrict the FLB’s ability to fully describe ground truth objects in mentions, in-dicating that additional tuning or decoding adjustments may be necessary to further improve Cover score. 

## C. Ratio of “The” in responses after applying FLB 

Since FLB adds first logit, which assigns a high probabil-ity to “The,” to the logits of subsequent tokens, we compare the proportion of “The” in the original responses and in the FLB applied responses using the 1,004 queries of the AM-BER benchmark. To further examine this effect, we conducted an ad-ditional GPT-4V based evaluation comparing baseline LLaVA-1.5 and FLB outputs across different γ values. Be-cause γ determines the maximum strength of the weighting function, we evaluated FLB with γ ∈ { 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7},where γ = 0 .3 corresponds to our optimized setting. Overall, these results indicate that FLB does not harm linguistic diversity or sentence quality despite influencing the distribution of sentence-initial to-kens.
