# GEASS: Training-Free Caption Steering for Hallucination Mitigation in Vision-Language Models

# Abstract 

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) excel at grounded reasoning but remain prone to object hallucination. Recent work treats self-generated captions as a uniformly positive resource, yet we find that naively embedding one can degrade rather than help—dropping Qwen2.5-VL-3B ac-curacy on HallusionBench by nearly 10 points. Two structural properties explain this. First, cap-tions anchor not only the model’s final answer but also its reasoning trajectory and lexical choices. Second, caption errors are asymmetric: omissions vastly outnumber fabrications, yet each fabrica-tion carries a much larger per-instance impact. A caption’s usefulness is therefore a per-query property, not a per-corpus one. We propose GE-ASS (Gated Evidence-Aware Selective Steering), a training-free module that decides on each query how much of the caption the model consumes: it gates the caption by the clean path’s confidence, weights it by the entropy reduction it produces, and raises the evidence bar when the two path-ways disagree. Experiments on POPE and Hallu-sionBench across four VLMs show that GEASS consistently improves over vanilla inference and contrastive decoding, with only two extra forward passes per query. 

# 1. Introduction 

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) (Wang et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b) have achieved remarkable progress on tasks such as visual question answering and grounded reasoning. However, VLMs suffer from a persistent failure known as object hallucination—generating responses inconsistent with the actual image content (Li et al., 2023; Augustin et al., 2025; Guan et al., 2024)—which undermines their reliability in

scenarios where factual grounding is critical. A key origin of this failure is the model’s query-driven at-tention pattern: when answering a specific question, VLMs focus locally and often fail to integrate evidence outside the question’s immediate scope (Huang et al., 2024; Tu et al., 2026). To address this limitation, two routes have emerged. The first route operates within the model, either refining training data and objectives (Liu et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024) or modifying the decoding process via contrastive decoding (Leng et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025) and attention reweighting (Tu et al., 2026). However, such methods redistribute the use of in-formation already attended to but introduce no new visual evidence, and thus cannot recover what the model never observed. The second route therefore introduces external 

information through image captions, which are generated under a generic “describe this image” instruction and glob-ally surface content that query-driven attention misses (Hu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2024a; Kapuriya et al., 2025). However, these works uniformly treat cap-tions as a positive auxiliary resource and feed them into the model unconditionally, leaving an open question: when, and to what extent, should a caption actually be trusted? 

The cost of unconditional trust is concrete. We observe that naively embedding a self-generated caption drops Qwen2.5-VL-3B (Bai et al., 2025) accuracy on HallusionBench (Guan et al., 2024)from 61.19 to 51.31 (Table 1)—a near 10-point degradation from a resource intended to help. The root cause is that captions are neither uniformly useful nor uniformly harmful but mixed-quality signals: captions are strong on global content and weak on details, while the model’s own answering is the opposite—so captions help on globally-oriented questions where they cover the model’s blind spot, but mislead on detail-oriented questions where their own omissions become the dominant error. Under such a back-ground, a question naturally arises: can we adaptively de-cide how much a caption should influence the model on each query, at inference time and without additional training?

A mechanism that operates at this level is orthogonal to caption-quality improvements, free of the overhead they carry, and directly addresses the mixed-quality nature of any realistic caption. Inspired by these findings, we propose GEASS (Gated 

Evidence-Aware Selective Steering), a training-free module that adaptively regulates how much a caption is allowed to influence the model at the logit level, rather than alter-ing the caption itself. GEASS runs two parallel forward passes—with and without the caption—and decides, on each query, the extent to which the caption may exert its influ-ence through three signals, each operationalizing one judg-ment derived from our observations: (i) a confidence gate 

(Gated ), derived from the clean-path maximum probability, proxies whether the answering pathway needs external help in the first place; (ii) an information-gain weight (Evidence-Aware ), measured by the caption-induced entropy reduction, proxies whether the caption is actually delivering useful in-formation for the current query; (iii) a disagreement penalty 

(Selective ) raises the evidence threshold when the two paths disagree, encoding the omission-over-fabrication prior so that a caption is granted greater authority to reinforce the model’s judgment than to overturn it. GEASS is plug-and-play, requires no architectural modification, and adds only two extra forward passes per query. It applies to any VLM that exposes logits during decoding and is compatible with standard decoding strategies. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • Caption-aided VLM inference is governed by two structural properties. We investigate how captions shape VLM behavior and identify the anchoring effect and the omission-dominated error asymmetry as the two properties that jointly determine when a caption helps and when it harms. • GEASS: adaptive fusion for mixed-quality captions. 

Rather than altering the caption, GEASS selectively modulates its influence at the logit level through three signals, each a computable proxy for one identified property, retaining the benefits of accurate captions while suppressing the harms of flawed ones with nearly zero extra overhead. • Plug-and-play capability and broad applicability. 

GEASS is validated on POPE and HallusionBench across four VLMs spanning different architectures and scales, achieving consistent gains over both vanilla inference and contrastive decoding baselines. 

# 2. Related Work 

2.1. Hallucination Mitigation in VLMs 

Object hallucination—where VLMs (Liu et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2024b) produce flu-ent but visually ungrounded content—has been documented across benchmarks targeting different aspects of the failure: yes/no polling on object existence (Li et al., 2023), halluci-nated object ratios in free-form generation (Rohrbach et al., 2018), and large-vocabulary stress tests (Augustin et al., 2025). Mitigation efforts split along training and inference axes. On the training side, prior work has curated targeted data (Liu et al., 2023) or applied RLHF (Sun et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024), all of which improve robustness at the cost of substantial compute and supervision. What unites this last line is a single assumption: the auxil-iary input being contrasted is uniformly degraded, and its in-fluence should be subtracted away . This assumption breaks down when the auxiliary input is itself partly informative—a setting we examine next. 

2.2. Captions as Auxiliary Information in VLMs 

Image captions are a natural way to inject the global view that query-driven attention misses, and have been used as auxiliary text across a range of VLM pipelines: as tex-tual connectors for black-box LLMs in knowledge-based VQA (Hu et al., 2023), as filtered summaries that re-duce noise before downstream consumption (Zhang et al., 2025), as higher-quality supervision during instruction tun-ing (Chen et al., 2024a), and as caption-aware fine-tuning for scientific VQA (Kapuriya et al., 2025). The shared premise across these works is that caption quality is the lever to pull: with a better captioner or a better filter, downstream performance follows. Two questions are conspicuously absent from this literature. First, none of these works asks how captions interact with the model’s own reasoning once embedded—whether their effect is bounded by information provision, or whether the model’s outputs become entangled with the caption’s phras-ing. Second, none asks what kinds of errors captions intro-duce, and whether different error types damage downstream performance asymmetrically. Our analysis in Section 3 shows that both questions have non-trivial answers, and that ignoring them is what makes naive caption augmentation backfire.

We investigate how captions actually shape VLM behavior, and identify two structural properties that jointly govern caption-aided inference: a deep anchoring effect (§3.2) and an asymmetric error structure (§3.4). Together they expose three failure modes that any inference-time mechanism con-suming captions must address (§3.5).

3.2. The Anchoring Effect 

Once a caption is embedded into the input, its influence extends well beyond the final answer. In Figure 1, the model reasons correctly without the caption but, once the caption is provided, lifts phrases such as “the outlet is now glowing and ready to supply power” nearly verbatim into its chain-of-thought, and flips its answer accordingly. The pattern holds at scale: across 100 random HallusionBench samples, unigram, bigram, and trigram overlap with the caption rises sharply for all four models tested (Table 2). The mechanism cuts both ways. Figure 3 shows the same im-age paired with two captions: a correct one (“a dog sitting on the beach”) leads both Qwen2.5-VL-3B and InternVL2-8B to revise an initially wrong No into Yes, while a fabricated one (“a cat. . . ”) drives the same two models to flip in the same direction and invent supporting details. Anchoring does not distinguish between truth and error—the caption decides the direction, and Table 1 reflects the cost when the direction is wrong: naively embedding self-generated cap-tions drops Qwen2.5-VL-3B accuracy on HallusionBench from 61.19 to 51.31. Per-instance impact, however, runs in the opposite direction. Across 100 paired instances (Figure 4), fabrication shifts the prediction toward the wrong answer by ∆p = 0 .64 on aver-age and flips the binary decision in 87% of cases; omission shifts it by only ∆p = 0 .13 and flips 11% . Fabrication is rare but each occurrence is near-fatal; omission is mild per instance but pervasive : multiple objects are typically dropped from a single caption, and the omission distribution, though centered near zero, retains a long tail that reaches the flip threshold. Neither error type alone could produce the 10-point drop in Table 1; the two accumulate through distinct channels—a few fabrications, each large, plus many omissions, each small. The asymmetry has a direct design consequence: a cap-tion’s reliability is not uniformly distributed across its text. Its positive assertions are highly trustworthy, while its si-lences carry little evidential weight. When the two pathways disagree—a caption attempting to overturn the model’s own judgment—the more likely culprit is a fabrication on the caption side, not an omission on the model side.

# 4. Method 

The three failure modes in Section 3.5 share a common structure: each is characterised by a distinct combination of two quantities the model exposes at decoding time—its own confidence on the clean path, and the change induced by the caption on top of it. We turn this observation into a method. GEASS performs two forward passes per query, with and without the caption, and combines their logits with a query-specific weight built from three components: a confidence gate that controls whether the caption is consulted at all, an information-gain term that measures how much the caption sharpens the prediction, and a disagreement penalty that raises the bar when the caption tries to override the model. # 6. Conclusion 

We studied how captions influence VLM inference and iden-tified two properties that govern the outcome: a deep an-choring effect, which extends a caption’s influence well past the final answer into the model’s reasoning trajectory, and an asymmetric error structure, in which omission outnum-bers fabrication but each fabrication carries a much larger per-instance impact. Because both properties operate on the same caption, mitigating one without the other is not enough. Acting on this, we proposed GEASS, a training-free mod-ule that decides, on each query, how much of the caption the model should consume. GEASS gates the caption by the clean path’s confidence, weights its contribution by the entropy reduction it produces, and raises the evidence bar when the two pathways disagree—a direct encoding of the omission-over-fabrication prior. The mechanism is plug-and-play, adds two forward passes, and applies to any VLM that exposes decoding logits. On POPE and HallusionBench across four models, it improves over vanilla inference and contrastive decoding without per-model tuning. GEASS treats agreement between the two pathways as evi-dence of caption reliability, which is correct when fabrica-tions are rare but breaks down when they are not—a regime where the disagreement penalty would suppress a genuine correction. Lifting this assumption, for instance through explicit object-level verification on the caption side, is left to future work.
