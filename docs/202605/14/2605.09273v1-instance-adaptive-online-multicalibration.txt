## Department of Computer and Information Sciences, University of Pennsylvania May 12, 2026 

Abstract 

We study online multicalibration beyond the worst-case. We give a single, efficient algorithm which dynamically interpolates between benign and worst-case sequences by adaptively refining a dyadic grid of prediction values. Its error is controlled by the number of leaves in the refinement tree. Our analysis recovers the known ˜O(T 2/3) worst-case-optimal rate for online multicalibration, while simultaneously automatically adapting to easier instances: in the marginal stochastic setting it obtains a rate of ˜O(√T ),and for piecewise-stationary means with J segments its rate is ˜O(√JT ). More generally, the rate depends on a threshold-complexity measure of the predictable mean process relative to the group family. We show that this dependence is tight up to logarithmic factors. 

# 1 Introduction 

Calibration is a basic statistical property that we want for probabilistic predictions to be “trustworthy”. At a surface level, calibration asks that predictions “mean what they say” in the sense that they should be unbiased predictors of the outcome, even conditional on the value of the prediction itself. For example, a calibrated weather forecaster should have the property that, amongst all days the forecaster predicts a 20% chance of rain, 20% of those days actually experience rain [Dawid, 1982]. Calibrated predictions are viewed as “trustworthy” because of how they mediate the interface between prediction and decision making: selecting an action that is a best response to calibrated forecasts is an optimal decision policy among all policies that map forecasts to actions, simultaneously for all sets of actions and utility functions [Foster and Vohra, 1997, Kleinberg et al., 2023, Noarov et al., 2025, Roth, 2022, Kiyani et al., 2026]. Remarkably, as first shown by Foster and Vohra [1998], it is possible to produce forecasts that are asymptotically calibrated even in sequential adversarial prediction settings, in which there is no underlying distribution, and outcomes can be chosen adaptively by an adversary. The rate at which algorithms can achieve calibration depends on the environment. In the standard batch/statistical learning setting, cali-bration can be obtained via marginal mean estimation: given T samples, simple algorithms can produce predictors whose expected calibration error, without the conventional 1/T normalization, scales as Θ( √T )

[Shabat et al., 2020, Gupta and Ramdas, 2021]. Calibration in the online adversarial setting, on the other hand, is a harder problem with the best known lower bounds of Ω( T 0.543 ) separating it from the stochastic setting [Qiao and Valiant, 2021, Dagan et al., 2025]. Regardless of the rate at which one achieves it, calibration alone guarantees surprisingly little: it is a marginal property, demanding unbiased predictions only on average across the full sequence. We instead consider guarantees which ensure predictions are calibrated simultaneously on each of many subsequences that can be defined by the data [Dawid, 1985, Lehrer, 2001, Sandroni et al., 2003]. The modern formulation of this idea—multicalibration [Hébert-Johnson et al., 2018]—is achievable at a rate of ˜O(T 2/3) in online adversarial settings [Noarov et al., 2025, Ghuge et al., 2025], and this rate is tight in both the sequential adversarial and batch settings [Collina et al., 2026a,b]. The online lower bounds, however— Ω( T 0.543 ) for 1

> arXiv:2605.09273v1 [cs.LG] 10 May 2026

marginal calibration [Dagan et al., 2025] and ˜Ω( T 2/3) for multicalibration [Collina et al., 2026b]—hold only in the worst case, leaving open whether typical instances admit substantially better rates. We ask: 

Are there sequential multicalibration algorithms that go beyond the worst case, achieving instance-dependent bounds that interpolate between O(√T ) rates on easy instances and the O(T 2/3) rates that are optimal in the worst case? 

We show that the answer is yes . There is a single, efficient algorithm that obtains (multi)calibration rates adaptive to the instance. It obtains ˜O(√T ) calibration rates on stochastic instances without groups (which is impossible for worst case adversaries), recovers the minimax-optimal ˜O(T 2/3) worst-case rate for polynomial-sized group families, and more generally smoothly interpolates between the two bounds as a function of how much the sequence of outcomes changes and how much the group structure can be used to non-trivially predict labels. For example, without groups, if the sequence of outcomes is produced by a piecewise-stationary environment in which the outcome yt is drawn from a distribution with mean qt which changes at most J −1 times across the T rounds, we show that our algorithm obtains calibration error scaling as ˜O(√JT ) — recovering the claimed stochastic bound as the special case in which J = 1 .This example and other marginal calibration guarantees are corollaries of our more general theorem for multicalibration for groups. The relevant “hardness" of a multicalibration instance is governed by how well one can predict the label means from group membership. This means instances in which label means change at every round might still be easy, so long as those changes are predictable from the context in a way that is “visible” to the groups on which multicalibration is defined. We give bounds in terms of a measure of instance complexity that quantifies how well thresholds of the adversary’s label means can be represented in the span of the multicalibration group functions. Like many online calibration algorithms, our algorithm maintains a discrete grid of prediction values and computes the minimax optimal strategy in a game defined by a weighting of the worst case next round bias. Unlike a majority of said standard algorithms, ours does not maintain a fixed set of prediction values. Instead, it adaptively refines the grid over time, refining each interval when the algorithm has played it many times. A run of the algorithm produces an adaptively “grown” tree of prediction values, and we show how to bound the algorithm’s multicalibration error as a function of the number of the tree’s leaves. Our results for different scenarios then follow from upper bounding the size of the tree that different sequences produce. 

## 1.1 Related Work 

There is a large literature on online multicalibration and closely related guarantees. The modern formulation of multicalibration is due to Hébert-Johnson et al. [2018], but the first online multicalibration algorithms predate this formulation [Lehrer, 2001, Sandroni et al., 2003, Foster and Kakade, 2006, Foster et al., 2011]. Gupta et al. [2022] gave the first efficient online multicalibration algorithm with rates scaling logarithmically with the number of groups, as well as generalizations to related guarantees (roughly speaking multicalibration for quantiles and moments). Lee et al. [2022] formulated online minimax multiobjective optimization and applied it to a variety of problems including multicalibration-style objectives, following the game theoretic tradition of deriving online calibration algorithms [Hart, 2025, Fudenberg and Levine, 1999, Foster, 1999, Foster and Hart, 2018]. Haghtalab et al. [2023] give an alternative unifying treatment of multicalibration algorithms through the lens of solving zero sum games with online learning algorithms. Farina and Perdomo [2026] interpret the kind of minimax step used in these papers as solving an expected variational inequality, and bring fast solvers to bear on it. Garg et al. [2024], Ghuge et al. [2025], Hu et al. [2025], Farina and Perdomo [2026] give oracle-efficient online multicalibration guarantees for rich benchmark classes. Their emphasis is oracle efficiency for large/continuous classes, whereas our algorithm is efficient only for explicitly represented finite group families and gives instance-adaptive rates. More recently, Hu et al. [2025] studied ef-ficient swap multicalibration for elicitable properties, giving algorithms online multicalibration for properties beyond means (c.f. Noarov and Roth [2023]). The minimax sample complexity of online multicalibration was recently settled by Collina et al. [2026a,b] in both the batch and online settings, showing that the worst-case upper bound given by Noarov et al. [2025] was tight. 2Bandit learning is another setting where there is a gap between stochastic and adversarial worst-case rates. There is a similarly motivated line of work that gives single algorithms obtaining the “best of both worlds” guarantees — i.e. optimal stochastic rates on stochastic instances without giving up on worst case rates on adversarial instances [Bubeck and Slivkins, 2012, De Rooij et al., 2014]. Similarly a branch of the online convex optimization literature studies more fine-grained regret bounds that adapt to properties of the realized loss functions, that can give better-than-worst-case o(√T ) regret bounds on well behaved instances (see e.g. [Chiang et al., 2012, Chen et al., 2021]). The idea of parameterizing the complexity of an adversary by how many times it changes strategies also appears in the literature on “tracking the best expert” [Herbster and Warmuth, 1998, Bousquet and Warmuth, 2002] and adaptive regret [Hazan and Seshadhri, 2007], which give regret bounds not just to the best expert in hindsight, but to the best sequence of experts that change a small number of times, or that have a small smoother measure of drift. Our adaptive refinement of prediction values is reminiscent of “zooming algorithms” for the Lipschitz bandits problem in which algorithms iteratively refine attention to more promising regions of action space [Kleinberg et al., 2008, Slivkins, 2011, Podimata and Slivkins, 2021, Krishnamurthy et al., 2020]. From this literature, Podimata and Slivkins [2021], Krishnamurthy et al. [2020] are the most closely related in that they give instance-dependent bounds. Recently, Hu et al. [2026] gave a swap regret algorithm for Lipschitz convex losses (where the action space is continuous) by using multiple scales of action discretizations. This is conceptually related to our approach; both approaches are designed to circumvent bottlenecks that result from algorithms tied to fixed discretizations of continuous action spaces. Despite the conceptual similarity, the techniques and results differ significantly; their algorithm simultaneously uses multiple discretization scales, and they prove worst-case regret bounds for different swap-regret objectives. Our algorithm adaptively refines predictions in a tree, and we prove beyond-worst-case-analysis style calibration bounds that take advantage of trajectory-specific structure.

Extension to multicalibration. The full multicalibration proof follows the same template, except that “approximately constant mean on a block” is replaced by “means approximated by a function whose threshold signs have low-weight group representations.” The informal reason is that the sleeping-experts wrapper gives bias control for every group–interval pair. Now fix a block S and a depth d, and suppose the predictable means μt are well approximated on S by a function f taking only a few values. If the midpoint rI of a depth-d interval is far from all of these values, then the sign of μt − rI is essentially sgn( f (xt) − rI ), the 7threshold function of f (xt) applied at rI . To prove that interval I cannot be played much, we want to control bias after multiplying by this threshold sign, which prevents cancellations between positive and negative bias. When that sign pattern has a low-weight representation as a linear combination of groups, this weighted bias is the same low-weight combination of groupwise biases, all of which the algorithm controls. Thus only intervals near one of the few score levels can be played often, with cost governed by both the number of score levels and the threshold-representation cost.
