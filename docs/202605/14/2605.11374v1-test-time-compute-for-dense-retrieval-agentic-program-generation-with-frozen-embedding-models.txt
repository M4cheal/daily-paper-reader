## Test-Time Compute for Dense Retrieval: Agentic Program Generation with Frozen Embedding Models 

Han Xiao 

Jina AI by Elastic 

han.xiao@jina.ai 

Abstract 

Test-time compute is widely believed to ben-efit only large reasoning models. We show it also helps small embedding models. Most mod-ern embedding checkpoints are distilled from large LLM backbones and inherit their repre-sentation space; a frozen embedding model should therefore benefit from extra inference compute without retraining. Using an agentic program-search loop, we explore 259 candi-date inference programs over a frozen embed-ding API across ninety generations. The entire Pareto frontier collapses onto a single algebra: a softmax-weighted centroid of the local top-K

documents interpolated with the query. This parameter-free default lifts nDCG@10 statis-tically significantly across seven embedding-model families spanning a tenfold parameter range, with held-out full-BEIR validation con-firming the lift on every model tested. 

1 Introduction 

Test-time compute has transformed reasoning in generative language models. Best-of-N sampling (Brown et al., 2024), self-consistency (Wang et al., 2023b), and verifier-guided search (Snell et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025) let a smaller model spend more inference FLOPs to match a larger one along a smooth Pareto curve. The common reading is that this scaling regime is reserved for large reasoning LLMs and that small models have nothing to gain. Dense retrieval, dominated by small embedding models, has correspondingly not had its test-time-compute moment. Frozen embedding models such as jina-embeddings-v5-text-small 

(j-v5-small ), e5-base-v2 , and 

gte-base-en-v1.5 are deployed with one forward pass per query and one cosine similarity per document. We argue that the small-model picture is mis-leading. Most modern embedding models are dis-tilled or initialised from large LLM backbones. 

Qwen3-Embedding (Zhang et al., 2025) starts from the Qwen3 foundation models and inherits their representation space. EmbeddingGemma (Vera et al., 2025) is built on Gemma 3 and uses its long-context backbone. j-v5 (Akram et al., 2026) is distilled from Qwen3 with task-LoRA adapters on top. SFR-Embedding-2 (Meng et al., 2024) is fine-tuned from Mistral-7B. NV-Embed-v2 (Lee et al., 2025) adapts a decoder-only LLM with latent attention. GritLM (Muennighoff et al., 2025) unifies genera-tive and embedding capabilities in a single Mistral-based model. To the extent that test-time compute is a property of the underlying LLM representa-tion space, these distilled small embedding mod-els should inherit at least some of that potential. Existing test-time scaling proposals for retrieval require an external generative LLM (Gao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a), a second supervisory retriever (Uzan et al., 2026), or trained extra param-eters (Xiao et al., 2026). This work asks a stricter question: how much can a frozen single-vector em-bedding model gain at inference, with no auxiliary model and no new parameters? We answer with an agentic program-search loop in which an LLM proposer writes Python programs over the frozen embedding API, a harness scores each candidate on a multi-task retrieval benchmark, and a lesson ledger accumulates ruled-out hypothe-ses as long-horizon memory. Section 3 describes the loop architecture. Section 4 reports the emer-gent structure of the search output. Section 5 val-idates the discovered programs on held-out full-BEIR tasks across seven embedding-model fami-lies and connects the winning program to classical pseudo-relevance feedback. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Test-time compute for generative LLMs 

Best-of-N scaling forms the dominant test-time compute paradigm for generative LLMs. Snell 1

> arXiv:2605.11374v1 [cs.LG] 12 May 2026

et al. (2025) formalize compute-optimal allocation between best-of-N sampling and iterative revision. Brown et al. (2024) show best-of-N scales cover-age log-linearly. Wu et al. (2025) establish a clean inference scaling Pareto frontier on math problem-solving. Wang et al. (2023b) introduce majority-vote over sampled chains-of-thought. Common to all four is stochasticity: sample many noisy candidates, then aggregate. We adopt the same question—whether extra inference compute can substitute for a larger embedding model—and find the embedding analogue is structural rather than stochastic. 

2.2 Generation-based query expansion 

HyDE (Gao et al., 2023) embeds an LLM-generated hypothetical answer in place of the query; Query2Doc (Wang et al., 2023a) concate-nates the LLM expansion to the query before encod-ing. Both rely on an external LLM at query time, trading inference latency and cost for retrieval gain. Zhuang et al. (2024) prompt LLMs to generate dense+sparse representations directly. We restrict ourselves to the embedding model itself, with no generative model in the inference path. 

2.3 Pseudo-relevance feedback 

Classical PRF (Rocchio, 1971; Robertson et al., 1995) re-formulates the query using terms from initial top-ranked documents. The de-facto sparse-PRF baseline is RM3 (Lavrenko and Croft, 2001), which interpolates the original query with a rel-evance language model estimated from pseudo-relevant documents and remains a strong baseline on top of BM25. Learned sparse retrievers such as SPLADE (Formal et al., 2021) likewise rely on lexical expansion. Recent dense PRF work takes three distinct routes. CEQE (Naseri et al., 2021) operates at the term level via contextualized embeddings. VPRF (Li et al., 2023a) aggregates top-k dense embed-dings into a vector-PRF query update. ANCE-PRF (Yu et al., 2021) trains a small reranker to consume top-k embeddings. VPRF is the closest training-free prior art, since it computes a uniform mean over the top-k retrieved-document embeddings and interpolates with the query. Our work can be seen as extending VPRF by replacing the uniform mean with a softmax-temperature-weighted centroid and adding an interpolation coefficient α; Section 5 validates this across 13 tasks and seven embedding-model families. A separate line of recent vector-PRF extensions brings an LLM into the inference loop, including LLM-VPRF (Li et al., 2025b), GPRF (Tu et al., 2026), and PromptPRF (Li et al., 2025a). These lie in a different inference regime than the training-free, no-LLM substrate explored here. PromptPRF reports that LLM-extracted features over PRF doc-uments narrow the gap between small and large dense retrievers. We observe a similar small-model lift without requiring an LLM in the inference path. The relationship between the programs discov-ered by our search and classical PRF is discussed in Section 6. 

2.4 Test-time scaling for retrieval 

MetaEmbed (Xiao et al., 2026) adds learnable Meta-Tokens that produce a flexible multi-vector representation, scaling accuracy with the number of tokens kept at test time. It requires training. GQR (Uzan et al., 2026) performs gradient descent on the query embedding using a second model’s similar-ity as supervision. It requires a secondary retriever. Both target multimodal hybrid retrieval. Late inter-action in ColBERT and ColBERTv2 (Khattab and Zaharia, 2020; Santhanam et al., 2022) is another way to spend more compute per query, but at the cost of multi-vector storage. Our regime is strictly more constrained, requiring a frozen single-vector embedding model, no second model, and no extra forward pass for the cheapest variant. 

2.5 Program generation and agentic discovery 

Closest to our methodological framing are LLM-driven program-generation loops in which a lan-guage model proposes candidate programs, an eval-uator scores them, and the surviving programs con-dition the next round of proposals. FunSearch (Romera-Paredes et al., 2024) discovered new con-structions for the cap set problem and bin packing this way. AlphaEvolve (Novikov et al., 2025) ex-tended the pattern to a coding agent capable of improving algorithmic primitives. ELM (Lehman et al., 2024) explored LLM-assisted evolution ear-lier. Our agent loop is an instance of the same pattern, specialized to embedding-program search. The proposer is conditioned on a Pareto frontier over cost and ∆nDCG, plus a structured log of which substrate families the search has already moved beyond. The evaluator is a pinned multi-task retrieval harness. To our knowledge this is the first time this loop has been applied to the dense-retrieval inference recipe. 2Proposer

3 Methodology 

The central contribution of this paper is a re-runnable agentic loop that discovers embedding programs for dense retrieval. 1 An LLM proposer explores a structured search space of embedding-level operations. A harness scores each candidate on a fixed multi-task retrieval benchmark under a GPU lock. A lesson ledger accumulates ruled-out hypotheses so the agent does not revisit struc-turally equivalent ideas. Section 4 reports what the loop produced across ninety generations of search, including the emergent algebraic structure of the Pareto frontier. Section 5 reports the cross-model validation at full-BEIR scale. 

3.1 Outer loop 

The agent runs as a cron-driven generation loop, with one full iteration laid out in Algorithm 1. At the start of each generation g a proposer reads three inputs: the current Pareto frontier Fg−1 over cost and average ∆nDCG@10; the per-cell eval-uation history over six universal cells, defined as two embedding-model sizes by three retained tasks {NFCorpus, SciFact, ArguAna}, with FiQA-2018 evaluated on every program as a separate stress task that does not gate frontier admission; and a struc-tured log of meta-lessons accumulated from prior generations. The proposer then writes one or two new programs as Python functions and appends them to a versioned registry v{g}.py . A separate runner process acquires a GPU lock, evaluates each newly registered program on all six universal cells over a small hyperparameter sweep, writes results to a JSONL ledger, and logs the outcome as HIT, MARGINAL, MISS, or non-universal back into the meta-lesson record for the next generation.

3.2 Proposer 

The proposer is an LLM agent that writes Python programs over the frozen embedding model API. It is bound by a four-rule contract. Each new program must touch a substrate family, a named category of embedding-space operations such as centroid replacement, LoRA fusion, or stochastic ensem-bling, that the lesson ledger does not mark closed. It must branch on argmax or argpartition or use only structurally fixed thresholds. It must cite at least one of six fixed proposal principles, such as “argmax over confidence, not over output values”. And it must justify why the program is not equiv-alent under composition to any program already in R. The structural-threshold rule prevents the proposer from quietly tuning hyperparameters into the program text. The agent is otherwise uncon-strained. It may manipulate text, introduce condi-tional gates, operate in score space, combine LoRA adapters, or compose operations in any order. The proposer is free to mutate hyperparameters of an existing program, recombine two parents, or open a new substrate family. Generation-by-generation behaviour is logged in PROGRAM_LOG.md .

3.3 Lesson ledger 

The lesson ledger is the loop’s memory. Each gener-ation appends which substrate families were ruled out, which compositions are redundant, and which families still have open hypotheses. Two repre-3sentative entries from PROGRAM_LOG.md , surfaced verbatim in the registry conditioning prompt, illus-trate the discipline. The first records that stochas-tic ensembling returns no signal on any of the six universal cells: the median of unit-norm embed-dings leaves the unit sphere and there is no verifier to pick a winning sample, unlike LLM best-of-N .The second records that raw-document HyDE with majority gates collapses by construction, because each top-k document viewed as a pseudo-query re-trieves itself with similarity one. The ledger keeps the proposer from revisiting structurally equivalent ideas under new names and is the principal rea-son the search remains productive across ninety generations. 

3.4 Evaluation harness 

Programs are evaluated on six universal cells, formed by crossing { j-v5-small , j-v5-nano }with {NFCorpus, SciFact, ArguAna}, plus FiQA-2018 as an additional stress cell on each embedding model. The four-task baseline reproductions in Ap-pendix Table 8 cover the same eight cells. Each cell runs MTEB v2.8.4 with a fixed dataset revision. Per-query nDCG@10 is logged so that paired boot-strap significance can be computed post hoc against any baseline. A program is called universal if all six universal cells are non-negative. Otherwise it is recorded but does not enter the frontier. The cost axis used to admit programs to the fron-tier is the per-query encoder forward-pass count. Per-query test-time complexity in dense retrieval decomposes into three components. Let F denote the encoder forward-pass cost, N the corpus size, 

d the embedding dimension, Nc the first-stage can-didate pool depth, and K the local centroid ra-dius. The trivial program P0 runs in O(F + dN )

time for query encoding and dense scoring, or in 

O(F + d log N ) when an ANN index replaces the dense scan. A frontier program at c× encoder cost runs in O(cF + dN c + dK ). This decom-poses into c encoder forward passes for the query and any re-encoded anchors, one O(dN c) matrix-vector multiply over the candidate pool to score against the updated query, and an O(dK ) aggre-gation to compute the centroid. The O(cF ) term dominates because F is roughly 10 to 50 ms per query for transformer encoders, while dN c is about 

0.05 ms at Nc=1000 and d=1024 on a single CPU thread. The cost factor c is therefore the meaning-ful test-time scaling axis. The agent’s frontier is admitted by the pair c and ∆nDCG@10 , with c

counted in encoder forward passes. We track three frontier strata. The 1× stratum requires no extra encoder pass and only matrix-vector arithmetic over precomputed embeddings. The 2× stratum allows one extra encoder pass for an anchor re-encoding. The ≥ 4× stratum allows multiple anchors or LoRA re-encodings. Within 

1× all costs are dominated by the O(dN c) matrix-vector multiply, which we treat as effectively free. Appendix B reports the empirical scaling of ∆

nDCG@10 against c across the registry of 1,522 evaluations. We use “encoder cost” and “encoder forward pass” as standard technical terms through-out, both referring to one forward pass of the em-bedding model. Section 4.2 reports the runtime trajectory of the loop, including the cumulative discovery curve and the substrate-family breakdown of programs pro-posed and ruled out. 

4 Agentic Discovered Programs 

The proposer agent is not constrained to a pre-defined grammar. Each program is an arbitrary Python function over a minimal interface: a frozen embedding model fθ mapping text to unit-norm vectors, a precomputed document index D with embeddings di = fθ(di), and retrieval via si =

fθ(q)⊤di. The agent is free to manipulate text through sentence splitting, truncation, and instruc-tion prefixes. It may introduce conditional branch-ing through gap-confidence gates, robustness wit-nesses, and overlap checks. It may operate in score space using Borda, RRF, or z-score fusion. It may combine multiple LoRA adapters. It may compose these operations in any order. This open-ended freedom is what makes the centroid-algebra find-ing non-trivial. The rest of this section reports the structure the agent independently re-derived as the simplest hypothesis consistent with the universal-positive criterion across all rounds of search.

4.2 Search trajectory 

We first examine the program-search trajectory to understand how programs evolve and what compute-accuracy tradeoffs emerge. The loop ran for ninety generations across roughly three wall-clock days, registering 259 pro-grams across seventeen substrate families and eval-uating 10,535 configurations. The proposer used Claude Opus 4 with a long-horizon prompt that in-cludes the current Pareto frontier and lesson ledger. One generation cost approximately 0.30 in API charges and produced one or two new programs. Total proposer cost was approximately $30 over the search. The cumulative discovery curve in Fig-ure 3 flattens after generation 30 and saturates after generation 78, suggesting the 2× universal frontier has been exhausted under the current encoder API. The trajectory reveals three distinct phases. The search began with the substrates most di-rectly inspired by LLM test-time compute. Cross-task LoRA fusion, dropout-based embedding en-sembles, asymmetric LoRA encoding, and multi-prompt query templates are the natural analogues of best-of-N sampling, self-consistency, and prompt engineering. All four were ruled out within the first ten rounds. The lesson ledger recorded the struc-tural reason in each case. Stochastic ensembling has no verifier to select among samples, so its mean leaves the unit sphere into a metric the model was not trained to respect. Asymmetric LoRA encod-ing projects queries and documents into misaligned subspaces, which removes the geometric alignment the embedding model relies on. The agent there-fore moved on quickly to substrates more aligned with the cosine geometry. The first successful programs emerged from embedding-space Rocchio, a classical technique the agent independently re-derived. The proposer then began systematic variation with similarity floors, multi-round iteration, z-score normalization, and negative feedback. It discovered that replac-ing the uniform mean with a softmax-temperature-weighted centroid and adding an interpolation co-efficient α produced a strictly better default. The SOFT CENTROID family crystallized around gener-ation 25 with K=3 , α =0 .5, τ =0 .05 .In the middle phase, the agent explored alterna-tive anchors including first-sentence re-encoding, title-only encoding, document chunking, corpus-mean subtraction, and pseudo-document replace-ment. FIRST SENT became the 2× frontier holder on ArguAna with gains of +6 .9 to 

+7 .3 nDCG@10. Score-space fusion methods and cascade reranking were explored and shown to be dominated by the centroid family. Once the cen-troid family was established, the agent turned to the question of when to apply programs, through gap-confidence gates, top-K overlap witnesses, and robustness witnesses. These gates modestly im-proved worst-case behaviour but did not move the universal frontier. The final twelve rounds covered instruction-prefix queries, document-side projection, title-as-anchor, and composite witnesses. They confirmed the saturation of the frontier identified earlier rather than extending it. The lesson ledger had accumu-lated 23 meta-lessons by this point, and the pro-poser’s novel-substrate rate dropped to zero, sig-nalling a clean stopping point for the search. A reader who only takes the winning program away from this paper will under-use the result. The frontier program is one realization of a search whose value lies in being rerunnable. Drop in a different embedding-model family, benchmark, or encoder API, and the same loop will produce a different frontier. 4.3 Branching structure 

The 259 programs form a directed acyclic graph of parent-child relationships, with each program cit-ing its parents and the mutation or recombination that produced it. The seventeen substrate fami-lies cluster into three super-families. The centroid super-family covers R OCCHIO , S OFT CENTROID ,FIRST SENT , R OCCHIO -FLOOR , pseudo-document replacement, and title-anchor variants. It accounts for over 180 frontier-eligible programs whose com-mon structure is a query update toward a local document centroid or anchor, varying in aggrega-tion, anchor source, and gating. The stochastic super-family covers D ROPOUT ENS , A SYM LORA, QUERY TEMPLATE , Q UERY REPEAT , and multi-prompt ensembling. Its 40 or so programs sit uni-formly at or below baseline because no verifier exists to select among noisy embedding samples. The score-space super-family covers Borda, RRF, z-score fusion, cascade reranking, and per-LoRA fusion. Its 30 or so programs are occasionally marginal but never universal, because operating in score space rather than embedding space loses the geometric structure that makes centroid up-dates effective. Representative frontier programs are schematized as flow diagrams in Appendix G. Of the 240 programs evaluated on the full set of six universal cells, 88 satisfy the strict universal-positive criterion of ∆ ≥ 0 on all six cells. Afurther 55 programs are positive on five of six cells, with mean ∆ from +2 .3 to +2 .7 nDCG@10. The near-universal cohort overlaps the universal-positive cohort in mean lift, suggesting that strict universality is a conservative admission criterion rather than a tight performance ceiling. The search produces a wide cohort of strong candidates above the trivial program P0, and the universal default we report is a conservative pick from this cohort. 

5 Experimental Results 

With the search trajectory and emergent algebra established, we now validate whether the discov-7ered programs generalize across tasks, embedding-model families, and corpus scales. 

5.1 Experimental setup 

The headline embedding models are j-v5-small 

and j-v5-nano (Akram et al., 2026), two recent task-LoRA models distilled from Qwen3 that ship four task-specialized adapters over a shared back-bone, one each for retrieval, classification, cluster-ing, and text-matching. This adapter design lets us also test cross-task LoRA fusion as a candidate sub-strate. Models use published HuggingFace weights in fp16. Evaluation uses MTEB v2.8.4 with the official task revisions (Muennighoff et al., 2023; Enevoldsen et al., 2025), and instruction prefixes follow the model card. The four headline retrieval tasks are NFCorpus (Boteva et al., 2016), SciFact (Wadden et al., 2020), ArguAna (Wachsmuth et al., 2018), and FiQA-2018 (Maia et al., 2018), which together span medical IR, scientific fact verifica-tion, counter-argument retrieval, and short-question to long-document financial QA, drawn from the BEIR collection (Thakur et al., 2021). Baselines reproduce published nDCG@10 within 0.4 nDCG points on all eight model-by-task cells (Appendix Table 8). All reported numbers use a two-stage pipeline. A first-stage ANN retriever returns a top-Nc can-didate pool that the embedding program rescores against q′. The discovered programs are insensi-tive to pool depth. Holding the default fixed and varying Nc ∈ { 100 , . . . , 5000 }, S OFT CENTROID 

reproduces the same ∆ nDCG@10 on all repre-sentative cells, because the centroid is built only from the local top-K. The rescoring cost is one 

d×Nc matrix-vector multiply, around 0.05 ms at 

Nc=1000 and d=1024 on a single CPU thread, negligible against the 10 to 50 ms encoder forward pass. Table 1 reports the oracle-best program per cell. To verify this is not overfitting, we run 5-fold random dev/test splits with a 50/50 ratio. The dev-selected S OFT CENTROID configuration retains 80% of oracle gain on j-v5-small NFCorpus, 67% on j-v5-nano NFCorpus, and 99% and 98% on the two ArguAna cells. On SciFact and FiQA, where oracle gains are below 0.7, dev-selected gains do not survive validation. We therefore re-strict claims to NFCorpus and ArguAna.

5.8 Regimes of test-time compute 

The experimental evidence across seven embedding-model families, thirteen retrieval tasks, and two held-out benchmarks converges on a unified geometric picture. The cosine paradigm is not erased by test-time compute, only wrapped. Three regimes emerge from the discovered frontier: the paradigm breaks on symmetric retrieval, bends on medical-IR head-lines, and holds on QA-leaning asymmetric tasks. Figure 5 summarizes this taxonomy across the four headline tasks and the two embedding-model fami-lies. The paradigm breaks on symmetric retrieval. On ArguAna, frontier programs gain six to seven nDCG@10 points over the cosine baseline on 

j-v5-small and j-v5-nano , with 99% retention under dev/test validation. On a second embedding-model family, the same default S OFT CENTROID 

gains over ten points on full-BEIR e5-base-v2 

at p < 0.0001 , as Table 3 reports. This is large enough to call the trivial program P0 dominated on symmetric retrieval across both embedding-model families. The break is enabled by the symmetry of the task: query and document are drawn from the same distribution, which makes “move q onto the document manifold” the right operation. The cosine geometry is not abandoned. The program exploits it more aggressively than P0 does. The paradigm bends on medical-IR headlines. On NFCorpus, the default S OFT CENTROID pro-gram gains +2 .0 to +2 .4 nDCG@10 with p < 

0.001 , and 67 to 80% of the oracle gain survives validation. The lift is real but bounded. It is large enough to be worth the two extra matrix multiplies and small enough that one cannot say cosine plus single-forward is dominated. The geometry is the same, used with a slightly larger program. The paradigm holds on QA-leaning asymmet-ric tasks for the centroid algebra alone, but the fusion lever from Section 5.6 closes much of the gap. Pure S OFT CENTROID on NQ and HotpotQA produces at most a neutral effect, with full-BEIR 

e5-base-v2 NQ yielding a small positive lift un-der K=3 . The structural reason is that the local top-K document centroid is dominated by lexical-overlap neighbours of the question. “What answers this question” is harder to estimate from the docu-ment manifold alone. Composing S OFT CENTROID 

with light BM25 score fusion moves these cells de-cisively. On e5-base-v2 13-task nanoBEIR, NQ flips from −0.06 under pure SC to +0 .93 under SC +fusion, a net +0 .99 , and HotpotQA flips from 

−2.58 to +2 .80 , a net +5 .38 . The QA limitation of pure centroid replacement is thus an address-able boundary rather than a fundamental ceiling, with sparse-dense fusion as the productive direc-tion. Lifting it further would call for an external generator or a task-specific adapter, both outside the training-free, LLM-free regime studied here. 

6 Discussion 

Our contribution is not that centroid replacement helps, which is consistent with classical PRF, but the positive separation between document-manifold programs and stochastic ensembling. 15 Among the seventeen substrate families explored, document-manifold programs form a distinct fron-tier dominating stochastic ensembling on every cell where they fire, with 99% of oracle gain retained on ArguAna under dev/test selection. ArguAna retrieves counter-arguments where query and docu-ment are drawn from the same distribution. Replac-ing q with the weighted mean of the top documents moves it onto the document manifold, exactly the right place under symmetric retrieval. The K=1 

setting that replaces the query with the top-1 docu-ment alone gives +6 .4 to +7 .1 on ArguAna while losing 3 to 4 points on asymmetric SciFact and FiQA. The agentic loop’s contribution is not the algebraic recipe, which is close to known PRF, but the autoresearch artifact. The de-fault K=3 , α =0 .5, τ =0 .05 ports across seven embedding-model families without a per-task hy-perparameter sweep, and the lesson ledger docu-ments which substrate families do not generalize. Rocchio remains a strong simple baseline. What the generation loop adds is the absence of grid-search overhead and a reusable scaffolding for re-running the search on any embedding model. The parameter sensitivity of this default is visualized in Figure 10 in Appendix D, which shows per-task lift across the α, K, and τ sweeps on e5-base-v2 .Practical deployment relies on approximate nearest-neighbour search rather than exact co-sine. We measure the lift’s robustness to HNSW (Malkov and Yashunin, 2020) parameter varia-tion on e5-base-v2 NFCorpus with two ANN calls per query, one for the initial top-K pool used to construct the centroid and one for the rescoring of the updated query q′. The lift is in-variant across efSearch ∈ { 16 , 32 , 64 , 128 , 256 }

with ∆ ∈ [+1 .45 , +1 .53] , efConstruction ∈{40 , 100 , 200 , 400 } with ∆ ∈ [+1 .44 , +1 .63] , and graph degree M ∈ {8, 16 , 32 , 64 } with ∆ ∈

[+1 .46 , +1 .56] . Every cell is significant at p < 

0.01 on the paired bootstrap. Centroid replacement therefore composes with off-the-shelf ANN stacks without parameter-specific tuning. End-to-end latency on the same NFCorpus in-dex measures 12 .53 ms baseline and 13 .27 ms with SOFT CENTROID on an NVIDIA L4 GPU, a +0 .75 

ms or +6% overhead. The corresponding CPU numbers are 48 .41 ms and 49 .49 ms, a +1 .08 ms or +2% overhead. The encoder forward pass dom-inates wall-clock in both regimes, and the second HNSW call plus centroid construction add sub-millisecond overhead. The pattern across the three regimes is that direct ports of the LLM TTC playbook diverge from the discovered frontier. D ROPOUT ENS , the best-of-N

analogue, sits at baseline. ASYM LORA, which samples under different LoRA conditioning, sits well below baseline because asymmetric LoRA misaligns the query and document subspaces. Gen-erative TTC wins because the output space is dis-crete and a verifier collapses a noisy distribution to a sharper mode. A dense embedding model’s output is a single point on the unit sphere Sd−1. Av-eraging M stochastic embeddings leaves Sd−1, and re-normalizing projects onto a Karcher-mean ap-proximation in a metric the embedding model was not trained to respect. There is also no inference-time verifier signal. What works is operations that respect the ranking algebra. Rocchio-style updates keep q near its trained location and nudge it toward the local centroid the embedding model already considers similar. Every frontier program leaves the cosine ge-ometry intact and uses extra inference compute to find a better point in it. This is the empirical ceiling under a frozen single-vector embedding model with no second model and no extra training. Lifting it will require either changing the embed-ding model, through multi-vector late interaction or trained TTC adapters, or admitting an external generator in the inference path. The value of the agentic loop relative to a fo-cused grid search over centroid variants deserves explicit discussion. A researcher who already knows that Rocchio-style updates are the right di-rection can define a grid over (K, α, τ, anchor ) and recover S OFT CENTROID in a few hundred eval-uations. The loop adds value on three axes that a focused grid cannot. First, the loop explores substrate families the researcher may not antic-ipate: 259 programs span 17 substrate families, and approximately 170 of those programs are anti-patterns, specifically LoRA fusion, dropout en-sembles, dimension gating, and score-space-only methods, whose falsification is itself informative. The lesson ledger accumulates these ruled-out hy-potheses so any future search on a new embed-ding model can skip structurally equivalent ideas without re-running them. Second, convergence to the centroid family was an emergent finding rather than a preset hypothesis. The loop started with LLM-TTC substrates and discovered centroid re-placement after those failed. A focused centroid 16 grid assumes the conclusion. Third, the lesson ledger is a reusable artifact: dropping in a different embedding model means the proposer skips the 170 known anti-patterns immediately, concentrat-ing evaluations on the open hypotheses. That said, if a researcher already knows that pseudo-relevance feedback in embedding space is the productive direction, a focused centroid grid will converge faster than the unsupervised loop. The loop’s comparative advantage is hypothesis generation under uncertainty, not parameter op-timization once the winning substrate family is known. The universality criterion’s gating set, defined as {NFCorpus, SciFact, ArguAna}, affects which programs are admitted to the frontier. We assess this sensitivity post-hoc using the e5-base-v2 13-task nanoBEIR evaluation from Section F. Replac-ing the gating set with {NFCorpus, FiQA-2018, SciFact} still admits S OFT CENTROID as univer-sal positive (per-task deltas +0 .58 , +1 .66 , +0 .55 ). Replacing it with {NQ, HotpotQA, ArguAna} ex-cludes S OFT CENTROID because NQ ( −0.42 ) and HotpotQA ( −1.31 ) are negative under pure cen-troid replacement; those cells are recovered by the BM25 fusion lever described in Section 5.6. Us-ing all 13 tasks as a strict gating criterion also ex-cludes S OFT CENTROID (8 of 13 positive). The original gating set therefore represents a conserva-tive but achievable coverage criterion that spans medical IR, scientific fact verification, and sym-metric retrieval, the three paradigms where pure centroid algebra produces reliable lift. QA-leaning gating sets would have directed the search toward hybrid programs earlier, but such programs were not fully within the training-free, no-LLM substrate constraint. The anchor choice for the 2× regime was ex-plored beyond F IRST SENT . Generation 89 of the search tested two title-field variants at 2× cost: P256 (title-only encoding as a robustness witness gate) and P257 (title as the interpolation anchor, replacing the first sentence). Both programs pro-duce positive lift on ArguAna across both j-v5 

model sizes (P256: +3 .78 small, +4 .54 nano; P257: +5 .20 small, +5 .26 nano), and P257 de-livers the second-largest 2× ArguAna gain after FIRST SENT . However, neither achieves universal-positive status: both programs remain MARGINAL on NFCorpus for j-v5-small , with gains of +0 .47 

and +0 .40 respectively that fall below the p < 0.05 

significance threshold. The lesson ledger records this as the structural reason: the curated title field carries the argument topic on ArguAna debate cor-pora but does not reliably signal medical-document relevance on NFCorpus, where the first sentence of the body carries the clinical framing that the title does not. F IRST SENT avoids this task-specificity by extracting the opening of the body rather than the metadata field. Alternative anchors such as last sentence or mid-document argmax sentence were also tested and either regressed on ArguAna or re-mained MARGINAL. Full investigation of anchor selection strategies is left to future work. 

7 Conclusion 

The common reading that test-time compute is re-served for large reasoning LLMs does not hold for the class of embedding models distilled from those same LLMs. A frozen single-vector embedding model admits a structured, model-family-agnostic class of test-time interventions whose discovery is the contribution of this paper. The boundary the search exposes is geometric rather than em-pirical. Stochastic best-of-N aggregators leave the unit sphere and lose the ranking algebra. Structured updates that move the query toward the local doc-ument centroid survive across seven embedding-model families at full-BEIR scale, with statistically significant gains on the symmetric and medical-IR headlines and held-out lifts on SCIDOCS and Touche2020. A complementary BM25 score fusion lever stacked on top of S OFT CENTROID broadens the gain across the wider nanoBEIR suite. The most useful artefact is not the winning pro-gram but the rerunnable loop, lesson ledger, and program registry that produced it. Drop in a differ-ent embedding-model family or benchmark and the same loop will produce a different frontier subject to the same geometric ceiling. The cosine paradigm is not erased by test-time compute, only wrapped. The remaining open question is whether that ceil-ing can be lifted from inside the cosine paradigm, or only by changing the embedding model.

H Limitations and Scope 

The proposer is itself an LLM agent and in-herits whatever priors the underlying model en-codes about which substrates are worth explor-ing. The explicit lesson ledger and the dev/test paired-bootstrap protocol partially mitigate this, but proposer bias cannot be eliminated. A different proposer could traverse the embedding-program space differently. The generation loop is a heuristic search and offers no coverage or optimality guar-antee. The lesson ledger reduces redundant ex-ploration but does not certify that the discovered frontier is globally optimal. Headline experiments use j-v5-small and 

j-v5-nano . Full-BEIR cross-model valida-tion extends to e5-small-v2 , e5-base-v2 ,

e5-large-v2 , gte-base-en-v1.5 , and 

bge-large-en-v1.5 for the fusion lever, as Table 6 shows. For iterative S OFT CENTROID 

on ArguAna and the Rocchio comparison, the same five models together with j-v5-small and 

j-v5-nano make up the seven-model cohort sum-marized in Tables 10 and 4. The optimal cluster size K is model-specific. bge-base-en-v1.5 

is partial and bge-small-en-v1.5 marks the parameter-scale boundary. The algebra is most reliable on models at or above one hundred million parameters. The multi-LoRA adapter design of j-v5 also enables substrate families such as LORAF USION and A SYM LORA that do not apply to monolithic embedding models, so those specific anti-patterns may not generalize. We evaluate on English BEIR. Performance on multilingual, conversational, or code-retrieval benchmarks is unknown. The 50-query nanoBEIR slices carry sub-sample variance; we recommend full-BEIR validation be-fore deploying any specific configuration.
