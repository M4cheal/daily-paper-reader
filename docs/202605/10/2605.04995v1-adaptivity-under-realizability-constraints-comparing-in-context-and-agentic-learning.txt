# Adaptivity Under Realizability Constraints: Comparing In-Context and Agentic Learning 

## Anastasis Kratsios ∗§ A. Martina Neuman †§ Philipp Petersen ‡§

## May 2026 

Abstract 

We compare in-context learning with fixed queries and agentic learning with adaptive queries for uniform approximation of task families. We consider two settings: an unrestricted regime, where querying and approximation are arbitrary functions, and a realizable regime, where we require these operations to be implemented by ReLU neural networks. In both settings, adaptivity never hinders approximation performance. However, this advantage can change when one passes from the unrestricted regime to the realizable regime. We identify four distinct approximation scenarios, each witnessed by an explicit task family: (a) no advantage of adaptivity; (b) an advantage in the unrestricted regime that persists under ReLU realizability; (c) an advantage that arises only under realizability; and (d) an advantage that disappears under realizability. This demonstrates that representational constraints interact profoundly with the effect of adaptivity. 

# 1 Introduction 

Modern learning systems exhibit well-documented few-shot [ 9, 33 ] and multi-task [10 , 6, 37 , 50 ] learning capabilities. These recent advances are largely attributed to their ability to access task information through prompts [ 9], retrieved examples [ 32 ], memory [ 41 ], or sequential interaction with an environment [ 53 ]. Fixed prompts are an instance of in-context learning (ICL), while sequential interaction leads to agentic learning .For a mathematically tractable treatment, we adopt a viewpoint in which sequential interaction consists of adaptive query selection based on past observations, whereas prompts are fixed queries specified a priori. The central question of this article is simple: when does sequential interaction actually help, and how does that answer change once realizability constraints are imposed? 

To this end, we compare four regimes, defined formally in Section 3: 

IC G (general in-context) , IC R (ReLU in-context) ,

AG (general agentic) , AR (ReLU agentic) .

For our purposes, an in-context learner is a function that can access a fixed (but arbitrary) collection of samples, whereas an agentic learner (or “agent”) selects its samples adaptively based on past observations. Both learners are required to approximate an entire given class of functions. Along a second axis, we distinguish between general approximators and ReLU-realizable (or simply realizable ) approximators. A general approximator is subject to no representational restriction; a realizable one requires both the querying

An immediate observation is the monotonicity

Here “ ≤” means that the method on the right does not incur a larger worst-case uniform approximation error than the one on the left. The inequality (1) follows from the fact that adaptive sampling can always be ignored, so an agent can imitate any fixed sampling rule. We formalize this fundamental fact in Proposition 4.1. In the main results, we isolate all three of these nontrivial configurations. Concretely, Theorems 4.1, 4.2, and 4.3 identify function classes realizing each of these patterns, which may be distinguished as follows: 

• adaptivity reveals more information : on cubical-path tasks, adaptive querying reveals strictly more task information than every fixed context, and this advantage survives the ReLU-realizability restriction; see Section 4.2; 

• adaptivity bypasses internal computation : on the pointed-value family, unrestricted in-context and unrestricted agentic learning are equally powerful, but a ReLU-realizable agentic learner can bypass a hard internal computation that a ReLU-realizable in-context learner needs to perform; see Section 4.3; 

• adaptivity is limited by hard computation : on the address-spike family, unrestricted adaptivity provides strict approximation gains, but now the informative adaptive query location is computationally hard to determine; consequently, bounded-size ReLU agents lose these gains; see Section 4.4. This yields the promised conceptual classification: once realizability is taken into account, adaptivity advantages can survive, appear, or disappear .

# 2 Related work 

In-context learning. Our notion of in-context learning is motivated by the modern few-shot prompting paradigm for large language models, where task information is supplied through examples or instructions at inference time rather than through parameter updates [ 9 ]. On the theoretical side, recent work studies what function classes can learn from such fixed in-context examples for structured families , typically exploiting shared low complexity across tasks, particularly in transformer architectures [ 2, 19 ]. While related in spirit, we use structured families from a different perspective: to highlight the role of adaptive information acquisition and to characterize how realizability alters its advantage. In our formulation, this corresponds to an agent ,possibly realized by a ReLU network, that adaptively selects which inputs to query and updates its predictor based on the observed responses. 2We distinguish our approximation-theoretic lens from the statistical lens that typically dominates the ICL literature. In particular, our focus differs from work on scaling laws [ 8], simplified attention mechanisms that learn linear tasks in context [ 51 , 55 ], the low-frequency inductive bias of trained transformers [ 52 ], and algorithmic perspectives in which ICL implements an implicit learning algorithm within the forward pass [ 49 ]or, more broadly, a meta-learning procedure that maps in-context examples directly to a predictor or its parameters [ 12 ]. These approaches operate on a given prompt of in-context examples and therefore do not address adaptive information acquisition or how it interacts with architectural constraints and realizability. 

Adaptive sampling, active learning, and information-based complexity. The adaptive component of our learning model is closely related to classical query learning and active learning , where a learner chooses which information to reveal based on past observations, viewed as sequentially acquired examples. Membership- and equivalence-query models already highlight the power of adaptive access to an unknown target through sharp bounds on query complexity , see e.g. [ 3], while the active learning literature studies how sequential sample selection reduces label complexity for generalization [ 14 , 15 ]. Adaptive sampling frameworks similarly study how measurements can be chosen on-the-fly to improve estimation accuracy for structured signal classes [ 11 , 47 ]. More broadly, our setting is most closely aligned with the framework of 

information-based complexity , which studies the intrinsic difficulty of approximation under partial information and restricted access to the target object. This framework was formalized in the 1980s in [ 40 , 48 ] and further developed in [ 39 ], where approximation under partial information is modeled via prescribed information operators (typically a collection of linear or nonlinear functionals), and complexity is measured by the minimal achievable error. Modern developments consider a range of information models, including limited bit encodings [22], linear measurements [45, 29, 1], and nonlinear (e.g., 1-Lipschitz) queries [13]. 

Multi-tasking and connections to operator learning. The shift from the classical universal ap-proximation perspective, where only a single task is approximated, to the approximation of task families remains only partially explored in ICL. This places our work within the broader context of approximation theory for operator learning , where many results admit an interpretation as “multi-task” approximation guarantees [ 18 , 24 , 23 , 30 , 27 , 34 ]. Those results, together with the first ICL approximation result of [ 33 ], suggest that multi-task learning may be subject to significant information-theoretic bottlenecks, similar to those encountered in neural operator approaches to simultaneously solving families of scientific computing problems [ 31 ]. The objective of our paper, however, is not to characterize worst-case scaling laws of arbi-trary multi-task approximation frameworks, but to isolate how adaptive information acquisition interacts with realizability constraints. This perspective extends classical results on non-adaptive approximation by MLPs [ 7, 36 ], suggesting that beyond function class complexity, the structure of information acquisition—such as adaptivity—also plays a central role. 

Approximation-theoretic setting. The contribution of this note is to compare two axes—adaptivity and ReLU realizability—within a uniform approximation framework. This allows us to study how adaptivity interacts with representational constraints, which is central to the phenomena studied here. Imposing realizability constraints, in turn, places the discussion in the well-studied setting of ReLU networks, where both upper and lower bounds are available; see [44] for an overview. We note that many of our results can be extended to multi-head transformers, and are therefore not MLP-specific, via our MLP-to-transformer conversion; see Proposition A.1 and the discussion in Remark 3.6. This mirrors [ 26 , Proposition 11] for our standard notion of attention, and is consistent with similar conversion results for CNNs [43], spiking neural networks [16, 38], and transformers [21].

Remark 3.4. We shall repeatedly use the following standard fact: for every compact domain X ⊆ Rd, every weight budget m, and every compact interval [a, b ], there exists a continuous function with values in [a, b ]

such that no ReLU network with at most m weights can approximate it uniformly with error strictly smaller than (b − a)/2. To see this, assume toward a contradiction that every continuous function f : X → [a, b ]

admits a ReLU network Φf with worst-case error smaller than (b − a)/2. Φa+( b−a)f (xi) > a + ( b − a)/2 if yi = 1 and Φa+( b−a)f (xi) < a + ( b − a)/2 if yi = 0 . This implies infinite VC dimension, contradicting [4, Theorem 8.4].

Remark 3.5. We focus on ReLU feed-forward neural networks because they provide a standard and well-understood baseline in approximation theory. Most of the qualitative arguments of this paper extend to other activation functions, provided three ingredients remain available: 

5• One should exclude pathological activations for which fixed finite architectures already become universal approximators; see [35]. 

• The activation should allow the construction of compactly supported bump functions as used in our separation examples. Higher-order ReLU activations are natural examples; for a general framework under fairly broad assumptions see [20]. 

• For the constructions in Section 4.3, one also needs efficient approximations to the multiplication operator. Remark 3.6. Typically, agentic methods are implemented with transformer architectures rather than feed-forward ReLU networks. However, ReLU network approximations can be translated into ReLU-transformers; see [ 17 ] and the quantitative version [ 26 , Proposition 11]. Here we use a slightly different notion of transformers compared to [ 26 , Proposition 11] and therefore demonstrate an adapted version of the transfer result in Proposition A.1. Hence, the in-context and agentic approximation results established here for ReLU networks also yield corresponding upper bounds for ReLU-based transformer architectures. To extend the separation statements in the same way, one would also need an unattainable target function for the relevant transformer class. It follows from [ 5, Theorem 8.14] that classifiers based on thresholding the output of transformers with ReLU activation functions have finite VC dimension. By the same argument as in Remark 3.4, this shows that for a fixed-architecture transformer there exists an unattainable target function.
