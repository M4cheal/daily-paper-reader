## Test-Time Compute for Dense Retrieval: Agentic Program Generation with Frozen Embedding Models 

Han Xiao 

Jina AI by Elastic 

han.xiao@jina.ai 

Abstract 

Test-time compute is widely believed to ben-efit only large reasoning models. We show it also helps small embedding models. Since modern embedding models are distilled from LLM backbones, a frozen encoder should ben-efit from extra inference compute without re-training. Using an agentic program-search loop, we explore 259 candidate inference programs over a frozen embedding API across ninety gen-erations. The entire Pareto frontier collapses onto a single algebra: a softmax-weighted cen-troid of the local top-K documents interpolated with the query. This default, which introduces no trainable parameters, lifts nDCG@10 sta-tistically significantly across seven embedding-model families spanning a tenfold parameter range, with held-out full-BEIR validation con-firming the lift on every model tested. 

1 Introduction 

Test-time compute has transformed reasoning in generative language models. Best-of-N sampling (Brown et al., 2024), self-consistency (Wang et al., 2023b), and verifier-guided search (Snell et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025) let a smaller model spend more inference FLOPs to match a larger one along a smooth Pareto curve. The common reading is that this scaling regime is reserved for large reasoning LLMs and that small models have nothing to gain. Dense retrieval, dominated by small embedding models, has not yet been studied through atest-time-compute lens. Frozen embedding models such as jina-embeddings-v5-text-small 

(j-v5-small ), e5-base-v2 , and 

gte-base-en-v1.5 are deployed with one forward pass per query and one cosine similarity per document. We argue that the small-model picture is mis-leading. Most modern embedding models are dis-tilled or initialised from large LLM backbones. 

Qwen3-Embedding (Zhang et al., 2025) starts from the Qwen3 foundation models and inherits their representation space. EmbeddingGemma (Vera et al., 2025) is built on Gemma 3 and uses its long-context backbone. j-v5 (Akram et al., 2026) is distilled from Qwen3 with task-LoRA adapters on top. SFR-Embedding-2 (Meng et al., 2024) is fine-tuned from Mistral-7B. NV-Embed-v2 (Lee et al., 2025) adapts a decoder-only LLM with latent attention. GritLM (Muennighoff et al., 2025) unifies genera-tive and embedding capabilities in a single Mistral-based model. To the extent that test-time compute is a property of the underlying LLM representa-tion space, these distilled small embedding mod-els should inherit at least some of that potential. Existing test-time scaling proposals for retrieval require an external generative LLM (Gao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a), a second supervisory retriever (Uzan et al., 2026), or trained extra param-eters (Xiao et al., 2026). This work asks a stricter question: how much can a frozen single-vector em-bedding model gain at inference, with no auxiliary model and no new parameters? We answer with an agentic program-search loop in which an LLM agent writes Python programs over the frozen embedding API, a harness scores each candidate on a multi-task retrieval benchmark, and a long-horizon memory accumulates ruled-out hypotheses. Section 3 describes the loop architec-ture. Section 4 reports the emergent structure of the search output. Section 5 validates the discovered programs on held-out full-BEIR tasks across seven embedding-model families and connects the win-ning program to classical pseudo-relevance feed-back. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Test-time compute for generative LLMs 

Best-of-N scaling forms the dominant test-time compute paradigm for generative LLMs. Snell et al. (2025) formalize compute-optimal allocation 1

> arXiv:2605.11374v2 [cs.LG] 13 May 2026

between best-of-N sampling and iterative revision. Brown et al. (2024) show best-of-N scales cover-age log-linearly. Wu et al. (2025) establish a clean inference scaling Pareto frontier on math problem-solving. Wang et al. (2023b) introduce majority-vote over sampled chains-of-thought. Common to all four is stochasticity: sample many noisy candi-dates, then aggregate. We adopt the same question: whether extra inference compute can substitute for a larger embedding model, and find the embedding analogue is structural rather than stochastic. 

2.2 Generation-based query expansion 

HyDE (Gao et al., 2023) embeds an LLM-generated hypothetical answer in place of the query; Query2Doc (Wang et al., 2023a) concate-nates the LLM expansion to the query before encod-ing. Both rely on an external LLM at query time, trading inference latency and cost for retrieval gain. Zhuang et al. (2024) prompt LLMs to generate dense+sparse representations directly. We restrict ourselves to the embedding model itself, with no generative model in the inference path. 

2.3 Pseudo-relevance feedback 

Classical PRF (Rocchio, 1971; Robertson et al., 1995) re-formulates the query using terms from initial top-ranked documents. The de-facto sparse-PRF baseline is RM3 (Lavrenko and Croft, 2001), which interpolates the original query with a rel-evance language model estimated from pseudo-relevant documents and remains a strong baseline on top of BM25. Learned sparse retrievers such as SPLADE (Formal et al., 2021) likewise rely on lexical expansion. Recent dense PRF work takes three distinct routes. CEQE (Naseri et al., 2021) operates at the term level via contextualized embeddings. VPRF (Li et al., 2023a) aggregates top-k dense embed-dings into a vector-PRF query update. ANCE-PRF (Yu et al., 2021) trains a small reranker to consume top-k embeddings. VPRF is the closest training-free prior art, since it computes a uniform mean over the top-k retrieved-document embeddings and interpolates with the query. Our work can be seen as extending VPRF by replacing the uniform mean with a softmax-temperature-weighted centroid and adding an interpolation coefficient α. VPRF is the 

τ → ∞ , α=1 limit of S OFT CENTROID and ap-pears as a data point in our ablation grid. Section 5 validates the full parameterization across 13 tasks and seven embedding-model families. A separate line of recent vector-PRF extensions brings an LLM into the inference loop, including LLM-VPRF (Li et al., 2025b), GPRF (Tu et al., 2026), and PromptPRF (Li et al., 2025a). These lie in a different inference regime than the training-free, no-LLM substrate explored here. PromptPRF reports that LLM-extracted features over PRF doc-uments narrow the gap between small and large dense retrievers. We observe a similar small-model lift without requiring an LLM in the inference path. The relationship between the programs discov-ered by our search and classical PRF is discussed in Section 6. 

2.4 Test-time scaling for retrieval 

MetaEmbed (Xiao et al., 2026) adds learnable Meta-Tokens that produce a flexible multi-vector representation, scaling accuracy with the number of tokens kept at test time. It requires training. GQR (Uzan et al., 2026) performs gradient descent on the query embedding using a second model’s similar-ity as supervision. It requires a secondary retriever. Both target multimodal hybrid retrieval. Late inter-action in ColBERT and ColBERTv2 (Khattab and Zaharia, 2020; Santhanam et al., 2022) is another way to spend more compute per query, but at the cost of multi-vector storage. Our regime is strictly more constrained, requiring a frozen single-vector embedding model, no second model, and no extra forward pass for the cheapest variant. 

2.5 Program generation and agentic discovery 

Closest to our methodological framing are LLM-driven program-generation loops in which a lan-guage model proposes candidate programs, an eval-uator scores them, and the surviving programs con-dition the next round of proposals. FunSearch (Romera-Paredes et al., 2024) discovered new con-structions for the cap set problem and bin packing this way. AlphaEvolve (Novikov et al., 2025) ex-tended the pattern to a coding agent capable of improving algorithmic primitives. ELM (Lehman et al., 2024) explored LLM-assisted evolution ear-lier. Our agent loop is an instance of the same pattern, specialized to embedding-program search. The agent is conditioned on a Pareto frontier over cost and ∆nDCG, plus a structured log of which substrate families the search has already moved be-yond. The evaluator is a pinned multi-task retrieval harness. To our knowledge this is the first time this loop has been applied to the dense-retrieval inference recipe. 23 Methodology

3.2 Proposer 

The agent writes Python programs over the frozen embedding model API. It is bound by a four-rule contract. Each new program must touch a substrate family, a named category of embedding-space oper-ations such as centroid replacement, LoRA fusion, or stochastic ensembling, that the long-horizon memory does not mark closed. It must branch on argmax or argpartition or use only structurally fixed thresholds. It must cite at least one of six fixed proposal principles, such as “argmax over confidence, not over output values”. And it must justify why the program is not equivalent under composition to any program already in R. The structural-threshold rule prevents the agent from quietly tuning hyperparameters into the program text. The agent is otherwise unconstrained. It may manipulate text, introduce conditional gates, op-erate in score space, combine LoRA adapters, or compose operations in any order. The agent is free to mutate hyperparameters of an existing pro-gram, recombine two parents, or open a new sub-strate family. Generation-by-generation behaviour is logged in PROGRAM_LOG.md .

3.3 Long-horizon memory 

The long-horizon memory is the loop’s persis-tent context. Each generation appends which sub-3strate families were ruled out, which composi-tions are redundant, and which families still have open hypotheses. Two representative entries from 

PROGRAM_LOG.md , surfaced verbatim in the registry conditioning prompt, illustrate the discipline. The first records that stochastic ensembling returns no signal on any of the six universal cells: the median of unit-norm embeddings leaves the unit sphere and there is no verifier to pick a winning sample, un-like LLM best-of-N . The second records that raw-document HyDE with majority gates collapses by construction, because each top-k document viewed as a pseudo-query retrieves itself with similarity one. The memory keeps the agent from revisiting structurally equivalent ideas under new names and is the principal reason the search remains produc-tive across ninety generations. 

3.4 Evaluation harness 

Programs are evaluated on six universal cells, formed by crossing { j-v5-small , j-v5-nano }with {NFCorpus, SciFact, ArguAna}, plus FiQA-2018 as an additional stress cell on each embedding model. The four-task baseline reproductions in Ap-pendix Table 10 cover the same eight cells. Each cell runs MTEB v2.8.4 with a fixed dataset revision. These nanoBEIR cells use 50-query sub-samples and serve exclusively for frontier admission during the search. All final claims are validated on full-BEIR corpora with paired bootstrap significance on per-query nDCG@10 using 10 ,000 resamples. A program is called universal if all six universal cells are non-negative. Otherwise it is recorded but does not enter the frontier. The cost axis used to admit programs to the fron-tier is the per-query encoder forward-pass count. Per-query test-time complexity in dense retrieval decomposes into three components. Let F denote the encoder forward-pass cost, N the corpus size, 

d the embedding dimension, Nc the first-stage can-didate pool depth, and K the local centroid ra-dius. The trivial program P0 runs in O(F + dN )

time for query encoding and dense scoring, or in 

O(F + d log N ) when an ANN index replaces the dense scan. A frontier program at c× encoder cost runs in O(cF + dN c + dK ). This decom-poses into c encoder forward passes for the query and any re-encoded anchors, one O(dN c) matrix-vector multiply over the candidate pool to score against the updated query, and an O(dK ) aggre-gation to compute the centroid. The O(cF ) term dominates because F is roughly 10 to 50 ms per query for transformer encoders, while dN c is about 

0.05 ms at Nc=1000 and d=1024 on a single CPU thread. The cost factor c is therefore the meaning-ful test-time scaling axis. The agent’s frontier is admitted by the pair c and ∆nDCG@10 , with c

counted in encoder forward passes. We track three frontier strata. The 1× stratum requires no extra encoder pass and only matrix-vector arithmetic over precomputed embeddings. The 2× stratum allows one extra encoder pass for an anchor re-encoding. The ≥ 4× stratum allows multiple anchors or LoRA re-encodings. Within 

1× all costs are dominated by the O(dN c) matrix-vector multiply, which we treat as effectively free. Appendix B reports the empirical scaling of ∆

nDCG@10 against c across the registry of 1,522 evaluations. We use “encoder cost” and “encoder forward pass” as standard technical terms through-out, both referring to one forward pass of the em-bedding model. 4 Discovered Programs 

The agent is not constrained to a predefined gram-mar. Each program is an arbitrary Python func-tion over a minimal interface: a frozen embed-ding model fθ mapping text to unit-norm vectors, a precomputed document index D with embeddings 

di = fθ(di), and retrieval via si = fθ(q)⊤di. The agent is free to manipulate text through sentence splitting, truncation, and instruction prefixes. It may introduce conditional branching through gap-confidence gates, robustness witnesses, and overlap checks. It may operate in score space using Borda, RRF, or z-score fusion. It may combine multiple LoRA adapters. It may compose these operations in any order. The centroid-algebra finding is non-trivial precisely because the search space is this unconstrained. The rest of this section reports the structure the agent independently re-derived as the simplest hypothesis consistent with the universal-positive criterion across all rounds of search.

4.2 Search trajectory 

We first examine the program-search trajectory to understand how programs evolve and what compute-accuracy tradeoffs emerge. The loop ran for ninety generations across roughly three wall-clock days, registering 259 pro-grams across seventeen substrate families and eval-uating 10,535 configurations. The agent used Claude Opus 4 with a long-horizon prompt that in-cludes the current Pareto frontier and long-horizon memory. One generation cost approximately 0.30 

in API charges and produced one or two new pro-grams. Total agent cost was approximately $30 

over the search. The cumulative discovery curve in Figure 3 flattens after generation 30 and satu-rates after generation 78, suggesting the 2× univer-sal frontier has been exhausted under the current encoder API. The trajectory reveals three distinct stages. The search began with the substrates most di-rectly inspired by LLM test-time compute. Cross-task LoRA fusion, dropout-based embedding en-sembles, asymmetric LoRA encoding, and multi-prompt query templates are the natural analogues of best-of-N sampling, self-consistency, and prompt engineering. All four were ruled out within the first ten rounds. The long-horizon memory recorded the structural reason in each case. Stochastic en-sembling has no verifier to select among samples, so its mean leaves the unit sphere into a metric the model was not trained to respect. Asymmetric LoRA encoding projects queries and documents into misaligned subspaces, which removes the ge-ometric alignment the embedding model relies on. The agent therefore moved on quickly to substrates more aligned with the cosine geometry. The first successful programs emerged from embedding-space Rocchio, a classical technique the agent independently re-derived. The agent then began systematic variation with similarity floors, multi-round iteration, z-score normalization, and negative feedback. It discovered that replacing the uniform mean with a softmax-temperature-weighted centroid and adding an interpolation co-efficient α produced a strictly better default. The SOFT CENTROID family crystallized around gener-ation 25 with K=3 , α =0 .5, τ =0 .05 .In the middle stage, the agent explored alterna-tive anchors including first-sentence re-encoding, title-only encoding, document chunking, corpus-mean subtraction, and pseudo-document replace-ment. FIRST SENT became the 2× frontier holder on ArguAna with gains of +6 .9 to 

+7 .3 nDCG@10. Score-space fusion methods and cascade reranking were explored and shown to be dominated by the centroid family. Once the cen-troid family was established, the agent turned to the question of when to apply programs, through gap-confidence gates, top-K overlap witnesses, and robustness witnesses. These gates modestly im-proved worst-case behaviour but did not move the universal frontier. 4.3 Branching structure 

The 259 programs form a directed acyclic graph of parent-child relationships, with each program cit-ing its parents and the mutation or recombination that produced it. The seventeen substrate fami-lies cluster into three super-families. The centroid super-family covers R OCCHIO , S OFT CENTROID ,FIRST SENT , R OCCHIO -FLOOR , pseudo-document replacement, and title-anchor variants. It accounts for over 180 frontier-eligible programs whose com-mon structure is a query update toward a local document centroid or anchor, varying in aggrega-tion, anchor source, and gating. The stochastic super-family covers D ROPOUT ENS , A SYM LORA, QUERY TEMPLATE , Q UERY REPEAT , and multi-prompt ensembling. Its 40 or so programs sit uni-formly at or below baseline because no verifier exists to select among noisy embedding samples. The score-space super-family covers Borda, RRF, z-score fusion, cascade reranking, and per-LoRA fusion. Its 30 or so programs are occasionally marginal but never universal, because operating in score space rather than embedding space loses the geometric structure that makes centroid up-dates effective. Representative frontier programs are schematized as flow diagrams in Appendix G. Of the 259 programs evaluated on the full set of six universal cells, 88 satisfy the strict universal-7positive criterion of ∆ ≥ 0 on all six cells. Afurther 55 programs are positive on five of six cells, with mean ∆ from +2 .3 to +2 .7 nDCG@10. The near-universal cohort overlaps the universal-positive cohort in mean lift, suggesting that strict universality is a conservative admission criterion rather than a tight performance ceiling. The search produces a wide cohort of strong candidates above the trivial program P0, and the universal default we report is a conservative pick from this cohort. 

4.4 Query-adaptive compute routing 

SOFT CENTROID applies the same centroid update to every query regardless of its difficulty or type. Since compute-optimal allocation outperforms uni-form allocation in LLM reasoning (Snell et al., 2025), we tested whether adaptive routing can fur-ther improve the embedding frontier. The agentic loop then explored 75 additional pro-grams across 26 further generations, seeded with the existing Pareto frontier and long-horizon mem-ory. The agent was instructed to vary the rout-ing policy rather than the algebraic primitive. The champion program, A DAPTIVE SC, classifies each query into one of three compute tiers based on two signals: query character length and the cosine-score gap between the top-1 and top-K retrieved documents (a confidence proxy). • Long queries (> 350 characters): double SOFT CENTROID pass followed by a first-sentence anchor amplification. Cost 1.5×.• Medium-length queries with high confi-dence : single S OFT CENTROID with a tail-gap boost. Cost 1.0×.• Short or low-confidence queries : standard SOFT CENTROID with no additional compute. 5 Experimental Results 

With the search trajectory and emergent algebra established, we now validate whether the discov-ered programs generalize across tasks, embedding-model families, and corpus scales. 

5.1 Experimental setup 

The headline embedding models are j-v5-small 

and j-v5-nano (Akram et al., 2026), two recent task-LoRA models distilled from Qwen3 that ship four task-specialized adapters over a shared back-bone, one each for retrieval, classification, cluster-ing, and text-matching. This adapter design lets us also test cross-task LoRA fusion as a candi-date substrate. Models use published HuggingFace weights in fp16. Evaluation uses MTEB v2.8.4 with the official task revisions (Muennighoff et al., 2023; Enevoldsen et al., 2025), and instruction prefixes follow the model card. The four head-line retrieval tasks are NFCorpus (Boteva et al., 2016), SciFact (Wadden et al., 2020), ArguAna (Wachsmuth et al., 2018), and FiQA-2018 (Maia et al., 2018), which together span medical IR, sci-entific fact verification, counter-argument retrieval, and short-question to long-document financial QA, drawn from the BEIR collection (Thakur et al., 2021). Baselines reproduce published nDCG@10 within 0.4 nDCG points on all eight model-by-task cells (Appendix Table 10). Frontier admission dur-ing search uses the nanoBEIR dev cells exclusively; held-out validation on full-BEIR corpora and un-seen embedding-model families guards against se-lection bias from the 10 ,535 evaluated configura-tions. All reported numbers use a two-stage pipeline. A first-stage ANN retriever returns a top-Nc can-didate pool that the embedding program rescores 8against q′. The discovered programs are insensi-tive to pool depth. Holding the default fixed and varying Nc ∈ { 100 , . . . , 5000 }, S OFT CENTROID 

reproduces the same ∆ nDCG@10 on all repre-sentative cells, because the centroid is built only from the local top-K. The rescoring cost is one 

d×Nc matrix-vector multiply, around 0.05 ms at 

Nc=1000 and d=1024 on a single CPU thread, negligible against the 10 to 50 ms encoder forward pass. Table 2 reports the oracle-best program per cell. To verify this is not overfitting, we run 5-fold random dev/test splits with a 50/50 ratio. The dev-selected S OFT CENTROID configuration retains 80% of oracle gain on j-v5-small NFCorpus, 67% on j-v5-nano NFCorpus, and 99% and 98% on the two ArguAna cells. On SciFact and FiQA, where oracle gains are below 0.7, dev-selected gains do not survive validation. We therefore re-strict claims to NFCorpus and ArguAna.

6 Discussion 

Our contribution is not that centroid replacement helps, which is consistent with classical PRF, but the positive separation between document-manifold programs and stochastic ensembling. Among the seventeen substrate families explored, document-manifold programs form a distinct fron-tier dominating stochastic ensembling on every cell where they fire, with 99% of oracle gain retained on ArguAna under dev/test selection. ArguAna retrieves counter-arguments where query and docu-ment are drawn from the same distribution. Replac-ing q with the weighted mean of the top documents moves it onto the document manifold, exactly the right place under symmetric retrieval. The K=1 

setting that replaces the query with the top-1 docu-ment alone gives +6 .4 to +7 .1 on ArguAna while losing 3 to 4 points on asymmetric SciFact and FiQA. The agentic loop’s contribution is not the algebraic recipe, which is close to known PRF, but the autoresearch artifact. The de-fault K=3 , α =0 .5, τ =0 .05 ports across seven embedding-model families without a per-task hy-perparameter sweep, and the long-horizon memory documents which substrate families do not general-ize. Rocchio remains a strong simple baseline. The generation loop eliminates grid-search overhead and provides a reusable scaffolding for re-running the search on any embedding model. The parameter sensitivity of this default is visualized in Figure 8 in Appendix D, which shows per-task lift across the α, K, and τ sweeps on e5-base-v2 .15 Practical deployment relies on approximate nearest-neighbour search rather than exact co-sine. We measure the lift’s robustness to HNSW (Malkov and Yashunin, 2020) parameter variation on e5-base-v2 NFCorpus. The lift stays within 

[+1 .44 , +1 .63] across wide sweeps of efSearch, efConstruction, and graph degree M , all signifi-cant at p < 0.01 . Centroid replacement composes with off-the-shelf ANN stacks without parameter-specific tuning. End-to-end latency on the same index is +0 .75 ms ( +6% ) on an NVIDIA L4 GPU and +1 .08 ms ( +2% ) on CPU, because the encoder forward pass dominates and the second HNSW call plus centroid construction add sub-millisecond overhead. The pattern across the three regimes is that direct ports of the LLM TTC playbook diverge from the discovered frontier. D ROPOUT ENS , the best-of-N

analogue, sits at baseline. ASYM LORA, which samples under different LoRA conditioning, sits well below baseline because asymmetric LoRA misaligns the query and document subspaces. Gen-erative TTC wins because the output space is dis-crete and a verifier collapses a noisy distribution to a sharper mode. A dense embedding model’s output is a single point on the unit sphere Sd−1. Av-eraging M stochastic embeddings leaves Sd−1, and re-normalizing projects onto a Karcher-mean ap-proximation in a metric the embedding model was not trained to respect. There is also no inference-time verifier signal. What works is operations that respect the ranking algebra. Rocchio-style updates keep q near its trained location and nudge it toward the local centroid the embedding model already considers similar. Every frontier program leaves the cosine geome-try intact and uses extra inference compute to find a better point in it. The adaptive-routing search (Sec-tion 4.4) demonstrates that the embedding TTC regime has the same fixed-versus-adaptive sepa-ration that characterizes LLM TTC (Snell et al., 2025): the frontier is not yet saturated once routing policy is added to the search space. This is the empirical ceiling under a frozen single-vector embedding model with no second model and no extra training. Lifting it will require either changing the embedding model, through multi-vector late interaction or trained TTC adapters, or admitting an external generator in the inference path. A researcher who already knows that Rocchio-style updates are the right direction can define a grid over (K, α, τ, anchor ) and recover S OFT CEN - 

> TROID

in a few hundred evaluations. The loop adds value on three axes that a focused grid cannot. First, the loop explores substrate families the re-searcher may not anticipate: 259 programs span 17 substrate families, and approximately 170 of those programs are anti-patterns, specifically LoRA fu-sion, dropout ensembles, dimension gating, and score-space-only methods, whose falsification is itself informative. The long-horizon memory accu-mulates these ruled-out hypotheses so any future search on a new embedding model can skip struc-turally equivalent ideas without re-running them. Second, convergence to the centroid family was an emergent finding rather than a preset hypothesis. The loop started with LLM-TTC substrates and discovered centroid replacement after those failed. A focused centroid grid assumes the conclusion. Third, the long-horizon memory is a reusable ar-tifact: dropping in a different embedding model means the agent skips the 170 known anti-patterns immediately, concentrating evaluations on the open hypotheses. If the productive direction is already known to be pseudo-relevance feedback in embedding space, a focused centroid grid will converge faster than the unsupervised loop. The loop’s comparative advan-tage is hypothesis generation under uncertainty, not parameter optimization once the winning substrate family is known. The universality criterion’s gating set, defined as {NFCorpus, SciFact, ArguAna}, affects which programs are admitted to the frontier. We assess this sensitivity post-hoc using the e5-base-v2 13-task nanoBEIR evaluation from Section F. Replac-ing the gating set with {NFCorpus, FiQA-2018, SciFact} still admits S OFT CENTROID as univer-sal positive (per-task deltas +0 .58 , +1 .66 , +0 .55 ). Replacing it with {NQ, HotpotQA, ArguAna} ex-cludes S OFT CENTROID because NQ ( −0.42 ) and HotpotQA ( −1.31 ) are negative under pure cen-troid replacement; those cells are recovered by the BM25 fusion lever described in Section 5.6. Us-ing all 13 tasks as a strict gating criterion also ex-cludes S OFT CENTROID (8 of 13 positive). The original gating set therefore represents a conserva-tive but achievable coverage criterion that spans medical IR, scientific fact verification, and sym-metric retrieval, the three paradigms where pure centroid algebra produces reliable lift. QA-leaning gating sets would have directed the search toward hybrid programs earlier, but such programs were 16 not fully within the training-free, no-LLM substrate constraint. The anchor choice for the 2× regime was ex-plored beyond F IRST SENT . Title-field variants produce strong ArguAna gains but fail to achieve universal-positive status on NFCorpus, because the curated title carries the argument topic on de-bate corpora but does not reliably signal medical-document relevance where the first sentence of the body carries the clinical framing. F IRST SENT 

avoids this task-specificity by extracting the open-ing of the body rather than the metadata field. 

7 Conclusion 

The common reading that test-time compute is re-served for large reasoning LLMs does not hold for the class of embedding models distilled from those same LLMs. A frozen single-vector embedding model admits a structured, model-family-agnostic class of test-time interventions whose discovery is the contribution of this paper. The boundary the search exposes is geometric rather than em-pirical. Stochastic best-of-N aggregators leave the unit sphere and lose the ranking algebra. Structured updates that move the query toward the local doc-ument centroid survive across seven embedding-model families at full-BEIR scale, with statistically significant gains on the symmetric and medical-IR headlines and held-out lifts on SCIDOCS and Touche2020. A complementary BM25 score fusion lever stacked on top of S OFT CENTROID broadens the gain across the wider nanoBEIR suite. The most useful artefact is not the winning pro-gram but the rerunnable loop, long-horizon mem-ory, and program registry that produced it. Sub-stituting a different embedding-model family or benchmark will produce a different frontier subject to the same geometric ceiling. The search demon-strates that the frontier is not static: query-adaptive compute routing, which gates on query length and retrieval confidence, universally improves over the fixed S OFT CENTROID default while remain-ing within the same centroid algebra. The cosine paradigm is not erased by test-time compute, only wrapped. The remaining open question is whether that ceiling can be lifted from inside the cosine paradigm, or only by changing the embedding model.
